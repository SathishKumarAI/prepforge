Bootstrapping estimates the uncertainty of a statistic by **resampling your data with replacement**, when you can't (or don't want to) rely on a formula. The procedure: from your dataset of size n, draw many resamples (1,000–10,000), each of size n *with replacement*; compute your statistic on each; the spread of those values is your empirical sampling distribution. From it you read off a **standard error** and a **confidence interval** — the percentile method just takes the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles for a 95% CI.

The core insight is almost cheeky: treat the sample as a stand-in for the population, and resampling *from it* mimics drawing fresh samples from the world. It works because a representative sample carries the population's shape.

Where it earns its keep: statistics with **no clean closed-form standard error** — the median, a correlation coefficient, a 90th percentile, the AUC of a model, the ratio of two metrics. For those, the textbook formulas either don't exist or rest on assumptions your data violates. Bootstrapping sidesteps that with almost **no distributional assumptions** — it's non-parametric — and quantifies uncertainty from a *single* dataset, which is often all you have.

The honest limits: it assumes your sample is representative, so it can't rescue a biased or tiny sample (with n=8 you're bootstrapping noise). It struggles with extreme-order statistics like the maximum, and it's compute-heavy — though trivially parallel. Worth knowing: this same "resample with replacement" idea is the **bagging** in random forests, so bootstrapping shows up in modeling, not just inference.
