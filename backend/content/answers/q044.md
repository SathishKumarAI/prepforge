First move: throw out accuracy. On 99% negative data a model that predicts "negative" always scores 99% and is useless. Switch to **precision, recall, F1, and PR-AUC**, and frame the problem around *which error costs more* — a missed fraud case vs. a false alarm — because that dictates everything downstream.

Then, roughly in the order I try things:

- **Class weights / cost-sensitive learning** — my first knob. `class_weight='balanced'` or `scale_pos_weight` in most libraries penalizes minority errors more, costs nothing, and often is enough. I reach for this before touching the data.
- **Resampling** — **SMOTE** to synthesize minority examples, or undersample the majority. SMOTE can help but also invents points in feature space, which sometimes creates noise; I benchmark against plain class weights rather than assuming it wins.
- **Threshold tuning** — the model outputs probabilities; the default 0.5 cutoff is arbitrary. Sliding the threshold along the PR curve to match your FP/FN costs is frequently the single biggest lever, and it requires no retraining.
- **Reframe** — at extreme imbalance (fraud, defects) treat it as **anomaly detection** rather than classification.

Two things that separate a correct answer from a naive one. **Resample inside cross-validation folds, on training data only** — oversampling before the split leaks near-duplicates into validation and gives you a fantasy score. And more data beats every trick — collecting more minority examples, if feasible, outperforms any resampling scheme.
