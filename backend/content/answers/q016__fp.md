question: "What is hallucination in LLMs and how can it be mitigated?"

To understand hallucination, understand what an LLM optimizes: the probability of *plausible* next tokens, not the *truth* of statements. Nothing in the training objective distinguishes "fluent and correct" from "fluent and false" — both are low-loss if they look like human text. A hallucination is therefore not a bug but the *default failure mode*: the model confidently emits a well-formed continuation that happens not to correspond to reality.

Why it's structural: the model has no internal ledger of "what I actually know" versus "what merely sounds right." Its knowledge is a lossy compression, so for facts it half-encoded, it *interpolates* — filling gaps with the statistically likely, which is often wrong. And because it's trained to be helpful and to always continue, silence or "I don't know" is under-rewarded relative to a confident guess.

Mitigations attack these roots: **grounding** via RAG supplies verifiable context so generation is anchored to sources; **prompting** for citations or "say if unsure" reshapes the distribution toward calibrated caution; **decoding** at lower temperature reduces risky sampling; **RLHF** trains the model to prefer honesty; and **verification** (tool use, self-consistency, external fact-checks) catches errors after generation.

The non-obvious insight: you cannot fully eliminate hallucination without solving calibration — teaching the model to *represent its own uncertainty*. Grounding reduces it; it doesn't cure the underlying blindness to truth.
