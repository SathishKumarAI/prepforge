question: "How does a decision tree decide where to split?"

A tree grows greedily: at each node it evaluates every feature and every candidate threshold, and picks the split that most improves node **purity** on the children. "Most improves" is measured by an impurity criterion.

For classification the two standard criteria are **Gini impurity** and **entropy/information gain**. Both measure how mixed the class labels are in a node; the split that yields the largest weighted drop in impurity across the two children wins. They usually agree — Gini is slightly cheaper to compute (no logarithm) and is scikit-learn's default. For regression, the criterion is **variance reduction** (equivalently MSE): pick the split minimizing the weighted variance of the target in each child.

Numeric features are handled by sorting values and testing thresholds between adjacent points; categoricals by testing subset groupings. The tree recurses until a stopping rule fires — `max_depth`, `min_samples_leaf`, or no split improves purity.

The thing worth knowing: this greedy, one-split-at-a-time approach is **locally optimal, not globally optimal**, and a fully grown tree will memorize the training set. Left unconstrained it overfits badly, which is why in production I essentially never ship a single tree — I use it inside **Random Forests** (bagging to cut variance) or **gradient boosting** (XGBoost/LightGBM, correcting errors sequentially). Also watch that impurity-based feature importances are biased toward high-cardinality features; prefer permutation importance or SHAP when the explanation matters.
