question: "What are Type I and Type II errors?"
topic: "Data Science"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"

---

Think of a smoke alarm. There are two ways it can be wrong. A **Type I error** is a false alarm: it screams when there's no fire. In statistics, that's declaring an effect real when nothing is actually happening — you wrongly rejected the "nothing's going on" assumption. Its chance is your significance level, **alpha** (often 5%).

A **Type II error** is the opposite: a real fire breaks out and the alarm stays silent. That's missing a genuine effect. Its chance is called **beta**, and "power" (1 minus beta) is the alarm's ability to catch true fires.

There's a trade-off: make the alarm less touchy to cut false alarms, and it misses more real fires. The best fix for both? A bigger sample — like adding more sensitive sensors. Which error hurts more depends entirely on the situation.
