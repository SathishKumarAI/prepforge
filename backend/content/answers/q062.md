Two ways a hypothesis test can be wrong. **Type I error (false positive):** you reject a null that was actually true — you "find" an effect that isn't there. Its probability is exactly your significance level **α** (0.05 means a 5% false-positive rate by construction). **Type II error (false negative):** you fail to reject a null that was actually false — you *miss* a real effect. Its probability is **β**, and **power = 1 − β** is your ability to detect a true effect.

The analogy that makes it stick: in a medical test, a Type I error tells a healthy person they're sick; a Type II error tells a sick person they're fine.

The part that matters in practice is the **trade-off**. Tightening α (say 0.01) cuts false positives but raises β — you'll miss more real effects. Loosen α and the reverse. You can't drive both to zero by fiddling the threshold; you're just moving error between the two buckets. **The one lever that reduces both simultaneously is a larger sample size**, because it increases power without touching α.

Which error to fear more is a business call. For fraud alerts that trigger costly action, a false positive is expensive, so keep α strict. In cancer screening, a missed case is catastrophic, so protect **power** — often accepting a looser α. The practical failure I see constantly: teams run underpowered A/B tests, get a null result, and conclude "no effect" when they simply lacked the sample size to detect one. Absence of evidence isn't evidence of absence — that's a Type II error in disguise.
