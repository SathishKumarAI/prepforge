An A/B test is a randomized controlled experiment — the point of the randomization is to make the two groups comparable so any outcome difference is *caused* by the change, not by confounders.

My design checklist:

1. **Hypothesis and one primary metric.** Pick a single success metric up front (e.g., checkout conversion). Deciding the metric after seeing data is how you fool yourself. Also define **guardrail metrics** (latency, revenue-per-user, complaints) that must not regress.
2. **Power analysis → sample size.** Before launching, compute the sample size from baseline rate, the **minimum detectable effect** you care about, α (0.05), and power (0.80). This tells you how many users and how long you need. Skipping this is why so many tests are inconclusive.
3. **Randomize** at the right unit — usually user, not session, to avoid a logged-in person seeing both variants. Verify assignment with a **sample-ratio-mismatch** check; a 50/50 split that lands 48/52 signals a bug.
4. **Run for full business cycles** (typically ≥1–2 weeks) to average out day-of-week and novelty effects — and commit to the duration.
5. **Analyze** with the appropriate test (two-proportion z-test for rates, t-test for means), reporting the **confidence interval on the effect**, not just a p-value.
6. **Decide** on statistical *and* practical significance, and check guardrails.

The failure mode I guard against hardest is **peeking** — checking daily and stopping the moment p<0.05. That inflates false positives because you get many bites at the apple. Fix the sample size in advance, or use a proper sequential-testing method built for continuous monitoring.
