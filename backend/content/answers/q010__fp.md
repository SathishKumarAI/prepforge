Language is humanity's richest information channel, yet it was engineered for humans, not machines: it's *discrete* (symbols), *ambiguous* (one string, many meanings), *compositional* (infinite sentences from finite words), and *context-dependent*. **NLP** is the discipline of bridging this gap — mapping between the symbolic, structured world of computation and the fluid world of human language.

The core difficulty is that meaning is not in the symbols themselves. "Bank" is identical bytes in "river bank" and "savings bank"; only *context* disambiguates. So every NLP task is fundamentally an exercise in recovering latent structure or meaning from surface form. This is why the field's breakthrough — **distributional semantics** ("a word is known by the company it keeps") — was so powerful: represent words as vectors whose geometry encodes meaning, learned from co-occurrence statistics.

Common tasks map onto levels of linguistic structure: tokenization and part-of-speech tagging (morphology/syntax), named-entity recognition and parsing (structure), sentiment analysis and classification (pragmatics), machine translation and summarization (generation), and question answering (semantics + reasoning).

The non-obvious insight: modern NLP collapsed these once-separate pipelines into a single objective — *predict the next token*. That one self-supervised task, at scale, implicitly forces a model to learn syntax, semantics, and world knowledge, because all of them help prediction.
