The ROC curve plots **true positive rate** (recall) against **false positive rate** as you sweep the classification threshold from 1 down to 0. Each threshold is a point; connecting them traces the trade-off between catching positives and raising false alarms. **AUC** is the area under that curve — a single number in `[0.5, 1.0]` where 0.5 is random guessing and 1.0 is perfect separation.

AUC has a clean interpretation I like to quote: it's the probability that the model ranks a random positive above a random negative. That makes it a measure of **ranking quality** that's independent of any specific threshold — useful when you haven't yet decided where to set the cutoff, or when comparing models.

The critical caveat: **AUC is misleading on heavily imbalanced data.** Because FPR has a huge negative denominator, a model can post an impressive 0.95 AUC while being nearly useless at the operating point you actually care about. For fraud, rare-disease, or ad-click problems — anything with a rare positive class — I report **PR-AUC** (precision-recall) instead, which stays honest when positives are scarce.

Two more notes: AUC says nothing about **calibration** (whether the probabilities are trustworthy), so pair it with a reliability curve or Brier score if you need real probabilities; and it doesn't pick a threshold for you — that's a business decision driven by the relative cost of false positives versus false negatives.
