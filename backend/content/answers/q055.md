Before choosing a technique, I ask *why* it's missing, because the mechanism dictates whether any fix is even valid. **MCAR** (missing completely at random) — the missingness is unrelated to anything, so deletion is safe. **MAR** (missing at random) — it depends on *observed* variables, so model-based imputation can recover it. **MNAR** (missing not at random) — it depends on the *unobserved value itself* (high earners refuse to state income), which is the dangerous case: no imputation is unbiased, and the missingness is itself a signal.

The toolkit:

- **Deletion** — drop rows or columns. Fine when missingness is tiny and plausibly MCAR; otherwise it throws away data and can bias the sample.
- **Simple imputation** — mean/median/mode. Fast, but it shrinks variance and distorts correlations, so I treat it as a baseline, not a finish line. Median over mean for skewed features.
- **Model-based** — kNN, regression, or **MICE/iterative imputation**, which respects relationships between features and is my default when accuracy matters.
- **Native handling** — XGBoost/LightGBM learn a default direction for missing values, so sometimes the best move is to *not* impute at all.

Two habits that separate careful work from sloppy work. **Add a missing-indicator column** — the *fact* of missingness is often predictive (a blank field can mean "user declined"), and you lose that signal by imputing silently. And **fit imputation on the training set only, inside the CV pipeline** — computing the mean over the full dataset leaks test information and inflates your score.
