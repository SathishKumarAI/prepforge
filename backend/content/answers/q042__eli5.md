question: "How does gradient boosting differ from bagging (random forests)?"
topic: "Machine Learning"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"
sources: []
---

Picture two ways to build a team of helpers. In **bagging** (random forests), you hire many helpers, each works independently on their own copy of the problem, and you average their answers. Everyone works in parallel and nobody talks. This mainly smooths out random mistakes, reducing **variance** (over-sensitivity to the exact training data).

**Gradient boosting** is more like a relay race with feedback. You add trees one at a time, and each new tree studies exactly where the current team is still getting things wrong — the leftover errors — and focuses on fixing those. Each contribution is added in small, careful steps. This mainly reduces **bias** (systematic mistakes), turning many weak guessers into one sharp predictor.

Boosting often wins on accuracy but needs careful tuning and can overfit; bagging is a sturdy, low-effort baseline.
