question: "What is bootstrapping and when is it useful?"

Suppose you have one modest sample — say 50 measured heights — and you want to know how trustworthy your average is. Ideally you'd go collect many fresh samples, but that's expensive or impossible. **Bootstrapping** fakes it cleverly: treat your 50 numbers like a bag of marbles, draw 50 *with replacement* (so some get picked twice, others not at all), and compute the average of that new pretend-sample. Repeat thousands of times.

Now you have thousands of slightly different averages. Their spread tells you how much your estimate would wobble across samples, letting you build error bars and confidence intervals — even for tricky quantities like the median, where no neat formula exists.

Its big appeal: it makes almost no assumptions about the data's shape. The catch: it trusts that your original sample fairly represents the world, so it struggles when that sample is tiny. It's also the idea behind "bagging" in ensembles.
