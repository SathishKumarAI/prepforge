The four types form a ladder of increasing value and difficulty: **descriptive** (what happened), **diagnostic** (why it happened), **predictive** (what will happen), and **prescriptive** (what to do about it).

Descriptive is the bread and butter — dashboards, weekly revenue, DAU/MAU, aggregations and rollups over historical data. Diagnostic goes a layer deeper: root-cause work, drill-downs, cohort and funnel breakdowns, correlation analysis to explain a dip. Predictive uses statistical or ML models — churn scoring, demand forecasting, LTV prediction — to estimate future outcomes. Prescriptive is the hardest and rarest: optimization and recommendation systems that actually tell you the action (reorder quantity, price, next-best-offer), often via simulation or constrained optimization.

The trap I've seen repeatedly in production is teams wanting to jump straight to predictive/prescriptive when their descriptive layer is still untrustworthy — broken pipelines, undefined metrics, no data quality checks. Get the foundation solid first; a churn model built on garbage event data just launders bad data into confident-looking predictions.

Practically, most business value still comes from rock-solid descriptive and diagnostic analytics done fast and reliably. Predictive and prescriptive are the differentiators once the basics are boring and dependable.
