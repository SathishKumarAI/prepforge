**Bayes' theorem** is a rule for updating your beliefs when new evidence arrives. You start with a hunch (the "prior"), see some evidence, and end with a revised belief (the "posterior").

Here's the eye-opener. Suppose a disease affects just 1 in 100 people. A test is 99% accurate at catching sick people but also cries "positive" for 5% of healthy people. You test positive — are you sick? Instinct says "99%!" But because the disease is rare, most people taking the test are healthy, so most positives come from that large healthy crowd's false alarms. Do the math and your real chance of being sick is only about **17%**.

The lesson: how common something is *beforehand* dramatically shapes what the evidence means. Ignore that base rate and you'll badly overreact to a positive. This same updating logic powers spam filters and much of probability-based AI.
