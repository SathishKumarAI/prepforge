Self-attention lets every token look at every other token and decide how much each one matters for building its own contextual representation. That's how a transformer resolves "it" to the right noun, or ties a verb to its subject across a long sentence.

Mechanically, each token projects into three vectors — **Query**, **Key**, **Value**. You score a token's Query against every token's Key (a dot product), scale by `√dₖ` to keep gradients stable, softmax the scores into weights that sum to 1, and take the weighted sum of the Values:

`Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √dₖ) V`

**Multi-head** attention runs several of these in parallel with different learned projections, so one head can track syntactic agreement while another tracks coreference. The heads' outputs are concatenated and projected back.

Two points I stress. First, unlike RNNs, attention connects distant tokens in a *single* step and processes all positions in parallel — that parallelism is why transformers train efficiently on GPUs and why they scaled. Second, the cost: attention is **O(n²)** in sequence length because every token attends to every other. That quadratic term is the real-world bottleneck behind context-window limits and the whole cottage industry of FlashAttention, sliding-window, and sparse-attention variants trying to tame it. In decoder LLMs, attention is also *causally masked* so a token can't peek at future tokens.
