Both are tree ensembles, but they're built on opposite philosophies. **Bagging** (random forest) trains many deep trees **independently and in parallel** on bootstrap samples, then averages them — the goal is **variance reduction**. **Boosting** trains trees **sequentially**, where each new tree fits the **residual errors** (the negative gradient of the loss) of the ensemble so far, and predictions are added with a small **learning rate** — the goal is **bias reduction**, turning many weak learners into one strong one.

The mental image: bagging is a committee voting at once; boosting is a relay where each runner corrects the previous one's mistakes.

That sequential correction is why gradient boosting — **XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost** — is the reigning champion on tabular data and wins a huge share of Kaggle competitions. But it comes with a bill:

- It **overfits if over-trained** (a forest basically can't), so you *need* early stopping and careful tuning of depth, learning rate, tree count, and regularization.
- It's **sequential**, so harder to parallelize than a forest (the libraries parallelize *within* tree construction, not across trees).

My rule of thumb: random forest is the robust, low-tuning baseline you can hand almost any tabular problem. Gradient boosting is where you go for maximum accuracy when you're willing to invest tuning time — with a validation set and early stopping wired up from the start, because an untuned booster will happily memorize the training set.
