question: "How can you estimate causal effects from observational data when you cannot run an experiment?"

The gold standard for proving something *causes* an outcome is a randomized experiment — flip a coin to decide who gets the treatment. Often you can't, though; you only have messy real-world data. The danger is **confounders**: hidden factors that sway both who got the "treatment" and the outcome, faking a link. (People who take vitamins may also exercise more — is it the vitamins or the exercise?)

**Causal inference** methods try to untangle this. **Matching** pairs treated and untreated people who look otherwise identical, imitating a fair coin flip. **Difference-in-differences** watches two groups over time and compares their *changes*. **Instrumental variables** lean on an outside nudge that affects treatment but nothing else. Each rests on assumptions — mainly that you measured the big confounders. Sketching the situation as a diagram of arrows helps decide what to adjust for. The deep frustration: you never see how one person would have fared both ways.
