**Clarify** — I'll confirm we mean Turing's 1950 "imitation game" and that the interviewer wants both the mechanics and a critique, since a nuanced answer scores higher than a definition.

**Approach** — Describe the setup, state precisely what it measures, then evaluate its validity.

**Depth** — A human judge holds text-only conversations with two hidden participants: one human, one machine. If the judge cannot reliably distinguish which is the machine, the machine passes. Crucially, it measures *behavioral indistinguishability in conversation*—whether a system can imitate human verbal responses—not consciousness, understanding, or reasoning. It's operational and functionalist: intelligence is judged by external behavior, sidestepping the unanswerable "can machines think?"

**Edge cases** — It's gameable: chatbots (ELIZA, Eugene Goostman) fool judges via deflection, misspellings, or persona tricks without genuine intelligence. It rewards deception and human mimicry. It ignores non-verbal intelligence (vision, robotics) and modern LLMs can be fluent yet wrong.

**Optimize & communicate** — I'd note alternatives: the Winograd Schema Challenge tests commonsense reasoning; capability benchmarks (MMLU) test knowledge. I'd close by narrating that the Turing Test's lasting value is philosophical—shifting the question from metaphysics to measurable behavior—which shows I understand not just what it is but why it mattered and where it falls short.
