Attribution confronts a genuinely hard problem: a conversion has **one outcome but many causes**. A customer saw an ad, clicked an email, searched, then bought. Credit must sum to 100%, yet the *true* causal contribution of each touch is unobservable—you can't rerun history minus one ad. So every attribution model is a **heuristic for dividing indivisible credit**, and each encodes a different assumption about how influence works.

- **Last-touch** — all credit to the final touch. Assumes only the closer matters. Simple, but blind to demand-creation.
- **First-touch** — all credit to the introduction. Assumes awareness is everything.
- **Linear** — split equally. Assumes every touch mattered the same (rarely true, but unbiased).
- **Time-decay** — more credit to recent touches. Assumes influence fades.
- **Position-based (U-shaped)** — heavy weight to first and last, less to the middle.
- **Data-driven / algorithmic** — infer weights from data (e.g., Shapley values, incrementality tests) rather than assuming them.

The unifying principle is **credit assignment**, the same problem reinforcement learning faces: rewards are delayed and shared across a sequence of actions. The Shapley value is the rigorous answer—average each touch's *marginal* contribution over all orderings.

The non-obvious insight: correlation-based models (all the rule-based ones) can't distinguish *influence* from *coincidence*. Only a **randomized experiment (incrementality/holdout)** measures true causal lift—which is why a channel can look great in last-touch yet drive zero incremental sales.
