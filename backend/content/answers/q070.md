Four metrics, and the real skill is knowing *which* to optimize for a given problem, because they encode different attitudes toward large errors.

- **MAE** — mean of `|yᵢ − ŷᵢ|`. In the target's units, easy to explain ("off by $12k on average"), and **robust to outliers** because errors aren't squared. It optimizes toward the **median** prediction.
- **MSE** — mean of `(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²`. Squaring makes it **punish large errors disproportionately**, and it's in squared units, so it's not directly interpretable. It optimizes toward the **mean** and is what most models actually minimize (differentiable, convex).
- **RMSE** — `√MSE`. Back in the target's units so you can report it, while keeping MSE's heavy penalty on big misses. The most commonly reported single number.
- **R²** — proportion of variance the model explains, `1 − SS_res/SS_tot`. 1 is perfect, 0 means "no better than predicting the mean," and it can go **negative** for a model worse than that baseline. It's unitless, which makes it good for communicating to stakeholders.

How I choose: if a few large errors are especially costly — demand forecasting where a big miss empties the warehouse — **RMSE/MSE**, so the model is pushed hard to avoid them. If the data has legitimate outliers I don't want dominating the fit — **MAE**. For explaining "how good is it" to a non-technical audience — **R²**, or **adjusted R²** when comparing models with different feature counts, since plain R² never decreases when you add features and will happily reward noise.
