The CLT says that if you take the **mean** of a large enough sample, the distribution of *that sample mean* — across many hypothetical samples — is approximately **normal**, no matter how weird the underlying population distribution is (as long as it has finite variance). The sampling distribution centers on the true population mean μ, and its spread — the **standard error** — is `σ/√n`, which shrinks as sample size grows.

The subtlety people miss: it's the distribution of the *statistic* (the mean) that becomes normal, **not the raw data**. Your income data can be wildly right-skewed forever; the average of samples of it still tends to normal.

Why it's the load-bearing theorem of applied statistics: it's *why* we can build **confidence intervals** and run **t-tests / z-tests** on the mean even when the data itself isn't normal. Without the CLT, non-normal data would block most classical inference. It's also the engine behind A/B testing — comparing two conversion rates leans on the sample means being approximately normal, which is exactly what the CLT delivers.

Two caveats I'd raise. "Large enough" is often quoted as **n ≥ 30**, but that's a rule of thumb — heavily skewed or heavy-tailed data needs much more, and for distributions without finite variance (some power laws) the CLT simply doesn't apply. And the standard error's `1/√n` is a hard truth about diminishing returns: halving your uncertainty requires **four times** the data, which is why chasing precision gets expensive fast.
