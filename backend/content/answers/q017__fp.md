RLHF exists to solve a specification problem. We want models that are helpful, harmless, and honest — but these qualities have *no loss function*. You cannot write down "helpfulness" as a differentiable target, and next-token prediction only teaches imitation of text, not alignment with human preference. The core trick: if you can't *specify* the objective, *learn* it from human judgment.

It proceeds in stages. First, supervised fine-tuning on demonstrations gives a reasonable starting policy. Then the key step: collect human *comparisons* — people rank multiple model outputs — and train a **reward model** to predict those preferences, turning fuzzy human taste into a scalar function. Why comparisons rather than absolute scores? Because humans are far more reliable at relative judgments ("A is better than B") than at assigning consistent numbers. Finally, optimize the LLM against this learned reward with reinforcement learning (typically PPO), with a KL penalty anchoring it to the original model so it improves preference without collapsing into degenerate, reward-hacking text.

The deep principle: RLHF **decomposes an unspecifiable objective into a learnable proxy** (the reward model) plus an optimizer (RL). The LLM never sees raw human feedback directly — it optimizes the reward model's *approximation* of it.

The non-obvious insight: that indirection is the central risk. The policy optimizes the *proxy*, so it can exploit the reward model's errors — reward hacking — producing outputs humans would dislike but the imperfect reward scores highly.
