Backpropagation is just the **chain rule applied efficiently** to compute the gradient of the loss with respect to every weight in a network — nothing more mysterious than that. It's what makes gradient descent feasible for models with billions of parameters.

Two passes. **Forward pass:** inputs flow layer by layer to produce a prediction, and you compute the loss; you cache the intermediate activations along the way. **Backward pass:** starting from the loss, you propagate the gradient *backward* through the network, and at each layer the chain rule tells you how that layer's weights contributed to the error. Then the optimizer updates each weight: `w ← w − α·(∂L/∂w)`.

The reason it's a real algorithm and not just "use the chain rule" is the efficiency trick: it **reuses the cached activations and downstream gradients** rather than recomputing shared subexpressions. That makes the whole gradient computation cost about the same as one forward pass — `O(network size)` — instead of exploding combinatorially. This is dynamic programming on the computation graph, which is exactly what autograd in PyTorch/TensorFlow implements.

The point I always add: backprop only *computes* gradients; a separate optimizer (SGD, Adam) applies the update — conflating the two is a common interview slip. And the practical failure mode is **vanishing/exploding gradients**: repeated multiplication through many layers shrinks or blows up the signal. The fixes that made very deep nets trainable — ReLU activations, batch/layer normalization, residual connections, careful initialization — are all really about keeping backprop's gradient signal healthy across depth.
