A KPI is a **Key** Performance Indicator, the small set of metrics that actually predict whether we're winning, not every number we can compute. I'd anchor on **Customer Obsession** and **Insist on the Highest Standards**: a good KPI ties to a customer or business outcome, not vanity activity.

I use SMART-style criteria: it must be **specific, measurable, actionable, tied to a goal, and time-bound**, plus it should be a leading indicator I can influence, hard to game, and have a clear owner and target. "Page views" is a vanity metric; "7-day activation rate" is a KPI because it predicts retention and I can move it.

I'd give a real example: I owned a support org's dashboard where leadership tracked ticket volume. I argued volume was noise and replaced it with "first-contact resolution rate" and "time-to-resolution p90," which tied directly to customer satisfaction. FCR rose from 61% to 78% over two quarters because teams now optimized the right thing.

A bar-raiser listens for whether I distinguish signal from vanity, tie the metric to the customer, and take ownership of moving it, not just watching it. Choosing the KPI is itself a high-judgment decision.
