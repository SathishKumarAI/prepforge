A **KPI (Key Performance Indicator)** is a metric tied directly to a business objective that you actively steer decisions by. The word that matters is *Key* — a metric becomes a KPI only when someone owns it, watches it, and changes behavior based on it. Everything else is just a metric on a dashboard.

A good KPI is **actionable, aligned to a goal, well-defined, and measurable on a cadence that matches the decision loop**. Actionable means a team can influence it — "stock price" fails this test for most teams. Aligned means it ladders up to a real objective (revenue, retention, efficiency). Well-defined means the exact SQL/logic is documented: "active user" needs a precise definition or two people will report two numbers.

The failure mode I've seen most is **vanity metrics** — cumulative signups, total page views — numbers that only go up and never inform a decision. Pair every KPI with context: a target, a comparison (week-over-week, vs. goal), and ideally a **counter-metric** to prevent gaming. If you incentivize "tickets closed," quality tanks unless you also watch reopen rate.

Frameworks like SMART or a **North Star metric** with supporting inputs help, but the real test is simple: if the number moved, would anyone do something differently? If not, it isn't a KPI.
