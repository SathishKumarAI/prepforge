question: "What is the difference between mean, median, and mode, and when is each useful?"

All three answer "what is a typical value?" but they optimize *different loss functions*, and that is the whole story. A summary statistic is the single number `c` that best represents the data under some penalty for being wrong.

- Penalize errors **quadratically** (`Σ(x−c)²`) and the minimizer is the **mean**. Because squaring magnifies large deviations, the mean must chase outliers to keep them from dominating — hence its famous sensitivity.
- Penalize errors by **absolute distance** (`Σ|x−c|`) and the minimizer is the **median**. Absolute loss weights a point by its direction, not its magnitude, so moving one point to infinity doesn't budge the balance point. That's *why* the median is robust — it's not a patch, it falls out of the loss.
- Penalize by **0/1 loss** (are you exactly right or not?) and the minimizer is the **mode** — the most frequent value, the only sensible "center" for categorical data.

So the choice is really a choice of what an error costs you. Use the mean for symmetric, outlier-free data (and because it supports algebra — it sums and updates cleanly). Use the median for skewed data like income or house prices. Use the mode for categories.

Non-obvious insight: mean − median ≈ a cheap skewness detector. If they diverge, the distribution is asymmetric and the mean is quietly being pulled toward the long tail.
