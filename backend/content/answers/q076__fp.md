The four types aren't an arbitrary list — they're the natural rungs of a ladder defined by the *question you ask of data*, and each rung requires strictly more than the one below it, in both information and difficulty.

- **Descriptive — "What happened?"** Compress raw events into summaries: totals, averages, dashboards. This is the base case; it needs only aggregation and imposes no model of the world.
- **Diagnostic — "Why did it happen?"** Move from summary to *explanation*: drill-downs, segmentation, correlation, root-cause analysis. It requires relating variables, not just counting them.
- **Predictive — "What will happen?"** Extrapolate learned patterns to unseen cases using statistical/ML models. This demands the strong assumption that past structure persists — you're now betting on stationarity.
- **Prescriptive — "What should we do?"** Recommend actions by combining predictions with objectives and constraints (optimization, simulation, causal reasoning). Hardest, because it needs *causal* understanding, not just correlation — you must know how the outcome responds to *intervention*.

The unifying principle: each step adds a layer of inference — observing, explaining, forecasting, intervening — and value rises with difficulty because acting on the future is worth more than describing the past.

The insight most miss: the jump from predictive to prescriptive is the real chasm. Prediction thrives on correlation, but *prescription requires causation* — recommending an action assumes you know what changes when you pull the lever, which patterns alone cannot tell you.
