question: "What do variance and standard deviation measure?"

A center (the mean) tells you where data sits; you also need to know how far it *typically strays*. So define spread as the average distance from the mean. But raw distances `x − μ` sum to exactly zero — the mean is the balance point, positives and negatives cancel. You must kill the signs first.

Two ways to do that: absolute value, or squaring. **Variance chooses squaring**: `Var = E[(X − μ)²]`, the mean squared deviation. Why squaring wins isn't arbitrary. Squaring is smooth and differentiable (absolute value isn't), which makes variance analytically tractable — it's the choice that makes calculus and least-squares work. More deeply, variance is the *unique* spread measure that is **additive for independent variables**: `Var(X+Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y)`. That property is the backbone of the CLT, error propagation, and portfolio theory. No other spread measure has it.

The catch: squaring changes the units — variance of a height in cm is in cm². **Standard deviation** `σ = √Var` simply takes the square root to return to the original units, so it's the interpretable one you report ("typically ±5 cm").

Non-obvious insight: variance is what the mathematics *loves* (additive, differentiable, the second moment), while standard deviation is what *humans read*. They're the same fact viewed through convenience versus interpretability — and squaring is exactly why variance is disproportionately sensitive to outliers.
