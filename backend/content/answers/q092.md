A time series decomposes into four components: **trend, seasonality, cyclical, and residual (noise)**.

- **Trend** is the long-term direction — the underlying upward or downward movement over the whole span, like steady user growth over three years. It has no fixed period.
- **Seasonality** is a **fixed, repeating pattern at a known period** — higher retail sales every December, traffic peaking every weekday morning, more signups every Monday. The defining property is that the period is regular and calendar-driven.
- **Cyclical** is often confused with seasonality but differs in one key way: cycles repeat over **variable, non-fixed periods** — business/economic cycles of a few years that don't align to the calendar.
- **Residual** is what's left after removing the above — irregular noise or one-off shocks (an outage, a promo spike).

The trend-vs-seasonality distinction is the one interviewers probe: **trend is direction, seasonality is a repeating calendar rhythm**. You separate them via **decomposition** — classical additive (`Y = Trend + Seasonal + Residual`) or multiplicative (`Y = Trend × Seasonal × Residual`, used when seasonal swings grow with the level), or STL for a robust version.

Why it matters operationally: you can't judge whether November was "good" by comparing to October — that's a seasonal artifact. Compare **year-over-year** or **deseasonalize** first. In production I've watched teams panic over a January drop that was pure seasonality every single year. Detecting a real trend change means stripping the seasonal component out first.
