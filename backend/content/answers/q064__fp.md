Ask a strange question: why is the bell curve *everywhere* — in measurement error, heights, test-statistic distributions — even when the underlying process is nothing like a bell? The Central Limit Theorem is the answer, and it reveals something almost thermodynamic about averaging.

The claim: sum (or average) many independent random variables with finite variance, and the *distribution of that sum*, properly rescaled, converges to a Gaussian — **regardless of the shape of the originals**. The individual quirks wash out.

Why must this happen? Averaging is a smoothing operation: each convolution of distributions pulls mass toward the middle and erases idiosyncratic features, and the Gaussian is the unique fixed point / maximum-entropy shape that survives repeated convolution under fixed variance. Odd moments cancel by symmetry of fluctuation; only mean and variance persist. It is an attractor, and information-theoretically the least-assuming distribution consistent with a fixed spread.

Why it matters: it's the license for nearly all classical inference. The sampling distribution of the mean is ~Gaussian, so standard errors, confidence intervals, and z/t-tests work *even when the population isn't normal*.

The subtlety people miss: it governs the **sum's** distribution, not the data's, needs finite variance (heavy tails like Cauchy break it), and convergence speed depends on skew — small, lopsided samples aren't rescued.
