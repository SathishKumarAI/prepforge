question: "What is the difference between a star schema and a snowflake schema?"

Both are dimensional models: a central **fact table** (measurable events — sales, clicks — holding foreign keys plus numeric measures) surrounded by **dimension tables** (the descriptive context — product, customer, date). The difference is how the dimensions are structured.

**Star schema:** dimensions are **denormalized** — each is one flat table hanging directly off the fact table. A `product` dimension carries category and department right in it, redundancy and all. Result: a query joins fact → dimension, one hop, so **fewer joins, faster queries, and simpler SQL** that business users can actually write.

**Snowflake schema:** dimensions are **normalized** into chains of sub-tables — `product → category → department`. This removes redundancy and saves storage, but a query now traverses several joins to reassemble a dimension, making it **more complex and often slower**.

My default is **star**, and it's not close for most analytics. Modern columnar warehouses (Snowflake, BigQuery, Redshift) make the storage savings from normalization nearly irrelevant — storage is cheap, compute and analyst time aren't — while every extra join taxes query performance and readability. Star schemas also map cleanly onto BI tools' semantic layers.

I'd only lean toward snowflaking a specific dimension when it's genuinely large with a deep, reusable hierarchy that's maintained in multiple places, where a single source of truth outweighs the join cost. In practice that's the exception. The interviewer is usually checking that you know fact vs. dimension, grasp the normalization-vs-join-performance trade-off, and can justify a default rather than reciting both. My default: **star, for query speed and simplicity.**
