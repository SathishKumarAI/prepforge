MLE is a principled way to fit a model's parameters: choose the parameters that make the observed data *most probable* under the model. Formally, you write the likelihood L(θ) = P(data | θ) — treating the data as fixed and θ as the variable — and pick the θ that maximizes it.

In practice you maximize the **log-likelihood** instead. The log turns a product of per-observation probabilities into a sum (numerically stable, no underflow from multiplying thousands of small numbers) and is monotonic, so the argmax is unchanged. You then solve ∂/∂θ (log L) = 0 analytically for simple models, or by gradient ascent otherwise.

The reason MLE matters is that a huge amount of ML is secretly MLE. Fitting **linear regression by minimizing squared error** is exactly MLE under Gaussian noise. **Logistic regression's log-loss / cross-entropy** is the negative log-likelihood of a Bernoulli model. So "minimize this loss" and "maximize likelihood of this probabilistic model" are usually the same statement.

MLE estimators are consistent and asymptotically efficient — with enough data they converge to the truth and hit the lowest possible variance. But they can **overfit on small samples** (that's the motivation for **MAP / regularization**, which is MLE plus a prior), and the likelihood surface can be non-convex with local optima. In production I lean on MLE's connection to loss functions to reason about *what* a model is actually assuming — a mismatch between the noise model and reality (e.g., squared error on heavy-tailed data) is a common, quiet source of bad fits.
