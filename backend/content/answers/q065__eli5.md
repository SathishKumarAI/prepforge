Imagine trying to guess the true average height of everyone in a city by measuring just a handful of people. Instead of announcing one number, you cast a net: "I'm 95% confident the real average is between 5'7" and 5'9"." That range is a **confidence interval** — a band around your estimate that probably contains the true value.

Here's the subtle part. The "95%" describes the *method*, not this one net. If you repeated the whole sampling-and-net-casting process many times, about 95% of those nets would catch the true value. It's *not* right to say "there's a 95% chance the true height is inside *this particular* net" — the true value is fixed; it's your net that jiggles around.

Narrow nets mean more certainty. You get them with bigger samples and less scatter in the data. A net tells you far more than a bare yes/no result.
