A point estimate from a sample is almost surely wrong by *some* amount — it's one draw from a distribution of possible estimates. The honest thing is to report not a guess but a *range* that reflects how much that estimate would wobble across repeated sampling. That is the confidence interval's purpose: quantify estimation uncertainty due to finite data.

Construction follows directly. The estimator (say the sample mean) has a sampling distribution with standard error SE = σ/√n. A 95% CI is estimate ± (critical value)·SE — a window scaled to capture the central 95% of that wobble. Note **√n**: halving the interval requires quadrupling the data. Uncertainty is expensive.

The interpretation trap, and the deep point: the 95% is a property of the *procedure*, not of any single interval. It means that if you repeated the whole experiment endlessly, 95% of the intervals you'd build would contain the true fixed parameter. A given interval either contains it or doesn't — probability 0 or 1; the randomness lives in the *interval's endpoints*, not the parameter.

The insight most miss: a CI is far more informative than a p-value because it shows both the *magnitude* and the *precision* of an effect. A wide interval straddling zero and a razor-thin one hugging zero are both "non-significant" yet mean opposite things scientifically.
