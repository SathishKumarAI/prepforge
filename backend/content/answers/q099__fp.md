question: "In an analytics context, how do you know if a change in a metric is statistically significant or just noise?"

Start from why the question even exists: any metric you measure is a **sample**, and samples fluctuate even when nothing changed. So the real question is *"could this observed change have happened by chance alone?"* Answering it requires a model of the noise itself.

That model comes from **sampling variability**. A rate measured on N users has a standard error that shrinks like `1/√N`. This is the crux: the *same* percentage change is noise at small N and a real signal at large N. You cannot judge a change without knowing how much random wobble to expect—which depends on sample size and the metric's variance.

The formal test compares the observed effect to that expected wobble. Under a **null hypothesis** (no true change), you compute the probability of seeing a difference this large by chance—the **p-value**. Small p (conventionally <0.05) means "noise alone rarely produces this." Equivalently, build a **confidence interval** on the difference; if it excludes zero, the change is unlikely to be noise.

But significance ≠ importance. Report the **effect size** and its interval, not just a yes/no—a trivially small change becomes "significant" at huge N.

The non-obvious insight most miss: **peeking and multiple comparisons silently inflate false positives.** Check a dashboard daily and you're running many tests; something *will* cross 0.05 by luck. Statistical honesty requires fixing the sample size (or using sequential methods) and correcting for how many metrics you scanned.
