question: "How does the k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN) algorithm work?"

Start from the most basic inductive assumption in all of learning: **similar inputs have similar outputs** (smoothness). k-NN is what you get when you take *only* that assumption and nothing else. To predict for a new point, find its k closest neighbors in feature space by some distance metric, and let them vote (classification) or average (regression).

Because it commits to no functional form, k-NN is **non-parametric** and "lazy" — there's no training beyond storing the data; all computation happens at query time. This directly implies its trade-off: it can approximate arbitrarily complex decision boundaries (high flexibility), but every prediction costs a search over the whole dataset, and it needs dense data to have meaningful neighbors.

k is the **bias–variance dial**. k=1 fits every point exactly (low bias, high variance — noisy, jagged boundaries). Large k averages over many neighbors (smoother, higher bias). You tune k to match the noise level.

The non-obvious insight — the **curse of dimensionality**: in high dimensions, distances concentrate, so the nearest and farthest points become almost equidistant. "Nearest neighbor" loses meaning, and the smoothness assumption silently breaks. This is why k-NN shines in low dimensions but degrades as features multiply — and why feature scaling matters enormously, since the distance metric weights every dimension equally by default.
