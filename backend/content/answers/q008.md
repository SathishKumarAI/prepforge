Minimax is the decision rule for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games (chess, tic-tac-toe, Connect Four). You build the game tree and assume both sides play optimally: one player (MAX) maximizes the backed-up score, the opponent (MIN) minimizes it. Values propagate up — MAX nodes take the max of their children, MIN nodes the min — and the move at the root leading to the best guaranteed value is chosen. Since you can't search to terminal states in a real game, you cut off at a depth and apply a static evaluation function to estimate leaf value.

Alpha-beta pruning is a pure optimization on top of minimax: same answer, far less work. It carries two bounds down the tree — **alpha** (best value MAX can already guarantee) and **beta** (best MIN can guarantee). When `alpha ≥ beta` at a node, the remaining children can't affect the outcome, so you prune them. With perfect move ordering this drops complexity from `O(b^d)` to roughly `O(b^{d/2})`, effectively doubling the depth you can search on the same budget.

That "with good ordering" caveat is the whole game in practice. I've seen engines get most of their strength from ordering heuristics — try the previous best move first (iterative deepening + a transposition table), then captures/killer moves. Add quiescence search to avoid the horizon effect at cutoff. Modern engines layer these on classic alpha-beta; it's still the backbone even alongside neural evaluation.
