Start from the constraint: an organization has limited attention and can optimize only a few things at once. A KPI (Key Performance Indicator) exists to *concentrate* that attention — it's a quantified proxy for an objective, chosen so that moving the number reliably means moving the goal. The word "Key" is doing real work: a metric you merely track is not a KPI; a KPI is one you *steer by*.

What makes one good follows from its job as a decision instrument:

- **Tied to an objective** — it proxies something you actually care about, not just what's easy to measure.
- **Actionable** — a team can influence it; otherwise it informs but can't guide.
- **Leading where possible** — it moves *before* the outcome (trial sign-ups) rather than only confirming it after (quarterly revenue), so you can course-correct.
- **Unambiguous and consistently defined** — one formula, one owner, so debate is about action not arithmetic.
- **Paired with a target and a time frame** — a number without a threshold can't say "good" from "bad."

The deep insight — Goodhart's Law: *"when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."* Any proxy can be gamed by optimizing the metric while abandoning the goal (calls closed fast, customers unhelped). So good KPIs are chosen for *robustness to gaming* and paired with **counter-metrics** — a KPI is a hypothesis about what drives value, not the value itself.
