The root problem is the curse of dimensionality plus finite data: every added feature expands the hypothesis space, so with fixed samples, irrelevant or redundant features give the model more ways to fit noise (variance) and cost interpretability and compute. Feature selection is really *capacity control* — pruning the input space so the signal-to-parameter ratio stays healthy.

Given that, there are only three structurally distinct places to intervene, and every method is one of them:

- **Filter methods** score features *independently of any model* — correlation, mutual information, χ², variance thresholds. Cheap and model-agnostic, but blind to interactions and to redundancy between features.
- **Wrapper methods** treat selection as search: try feature subsets, train the model, keep what improves validation score (forward/backward selection, RFE). They capture interactions and match the actual model, but the subset space is 2^p — combinatorial and prone to overfitting the validation set.
- **Embedded methods** fold selection *into* training. L1/Lasso pushes weights to exactly zero (geometry: the corner of the diamond constraint touches sparse solutions); tree ensembles rank by impurity reduction. Best cost/quality trade-off since selection and fitting share one objective.

The insight people miss: a "weak" feature alone can be powerful *jointly*, and a strong-looking one can be pure leakage. Univariate filters can't see either — always validate selection inside cross-validation to avoid leaking the target.
