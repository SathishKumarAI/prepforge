The distinction is about *which optimization loop owns the variable*. Parameters (weights, split thresholds) are chosen by the **inner loop** — the training algorithm minimizing loss on the data via gradient descent or a closed form. Hyperparameters (learning rate, tree depth, regularization `λ`, number of layers) are chosen by an **outer loop** — they define the model's capacity and the shape of the inner optimization itself.

Why can't we just learn hyperparameters too? Because they often trade *fit against generalization*, and generalization isn't measurable on the training set. Minimizing training loss over `λ` would drive `λ→0` — maximal overfitting. So hyperparameters must be selected against **held-out data**, which is why they need a separate loop and a validation set.

This makes tuning a search over a black-box function: "hyperparameters → validation score," which has no gradient and is expensive to evaluate. Grid search is exhaustive but scales exponentially. **Random search** is usually better because performance often depends on only a few hyperparameters, and random sampling covers those dimensions more densely per trial. **Bayesian optimization** builds a surrogate model of the score surface to propose promising points, spending evaluations where they matter.

Non-obvious insight: hyperparameters are just parameters at a different altitude — separated only because we lack a differentiable, non-cheating signal to learn them jointly.
