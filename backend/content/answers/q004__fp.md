Begin with why the abstraction exists. To study intelligence scientifically you need a boundary — something that separates "the thing being intelligent" from "the world it acts on." The **agent** is exactly that boundary: anything that *perceives* its environment through sensors and *acts* upon it through actuators. The interface is a loop: percept in, action out, repeat.

Why must it work this way? Because intelligence is only meaningful relative to a goal and an environment. An agent is formally a function mapping percept histories to actions. The interesting content lives in *how* that function is chosen — and here the deep principle is **rationality**: a rational agent selects the action that maximizes expected performance given its knowledge. This connects directly to decision theory and the maximization of expected utility.

The taxonomy (simple reflex, model-based, goal-based, utility-based, learning) is really a ladder of how much of the world the agent internally represents — from stateless reflexes to agents that model dynamics and reason over consequences.

The non-obvious insight: rationality is *bounded by the percept sequence and computation available* — an agent isn't irrational for failing at what it couldn't perceive or afford to compute. Intelligence is optimization under an information-and-resource budget, not omniscience.
