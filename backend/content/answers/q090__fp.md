question: "What is the difference between OLTP and OLAP systems?"

Two workloads pull the hardware in opposite directions, and the whole distinction falls out of that conflict.

**OLTP** (Online Transaction Processing) runs the business *as it happens*: many concurrent users each touching a few rows—place an order, update a balance. Its cost function is *latency per small operation* and *correctness under concurrency*. That dictates **row-oriented storage** (a whole record sits together, so reading/writing one entity hits one place), heavy indexing for point lookups, normalization to avoid update anomalies, and ACID transactions to keep concurrent writes consistent.

**OLAP** (Online Analytical Processing) answers questions *about* the business: few users, each scanning millions of rows across few columns—"revenue by region by quarter." Its cost function is *throughput of massive aggregations*. That dictates **column-oriented storage** (scan only the columns you need, and like-values compress brilliantly), denormalized star schemas to avoid joins, and read-optimized, append-mostly loads.

The unifying principle: **you cannot store bytes in a layout that is simultaneously optimal for row-at-a-time writes and column-at-a-time scans.** Physical data layout forces a choice, and each system picks the opposite corner of that trade-off.

The non-obvious insight: this is why you *separate* them and pipe data OLTP→OLAP via ETL. Running heavy analytics on the transactional store isn't just slow—the scans lock and thrash the very system keeping the business running.
