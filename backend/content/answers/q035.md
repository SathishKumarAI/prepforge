Expected test error decomposes into three parts: `Error = Bias² + Variance + Irreducible noise`. **Bias** is error from wrong assumptions — the model is too simple to capture the pattern (fitting a line to a curve); high bias means **underfitting**. **Variance** is error from sensitivity to the particular training set — the model chases noise; high variance means **overfitting**. The irreducible term is inherent randomness you can't beat.

The trade-off: dialing up model complexity lowers bias but raises variance, and vice versa. A depth-1 decision tree is high-bias/low-variance; a fully grown tree is the reverse. The goal isn't zero of either — it's the total-error minimum in between.

How I actually *read* it from curves: high training error *and* high validation error → high bias, so add capacity or better features. Low training error but a big gap to validation → high variance, so regularize, get more data, or simplify. That diagnosis directly tells you what to do next, which is why it's such a useful mental model.

Two nuances worth raising. Ensembles let you attack one term without paying the other: **bagging** (random forests) cuts variance, **boosting** cuts bias. And the classic U-shaped curve isn't the whole story anymore — modern **over-parameterized** deep nets exhibit **double descent**, where pushing well past the interpolation point makes test error *fall again*. So the trade-off is the right intuition for classical models and a useful starting point for deep ones, not an iron law.
