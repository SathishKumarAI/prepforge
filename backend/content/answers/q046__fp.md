Among the infinitely many hyperplanes that separate two classes, which should you trust to generalize? The SVM answer follows from a robustness principle: pick the plane with the **widest margin** — the largest empty corridor between the classes. Intuitively, the most confident boundary is the one farthest from every point, so small perturbations won't flip a label.

Maximizing the margin `2/‖w‖` becomes minimizing `‖w‖²` subject to every point being correctly classified with margin ≥1. This convex problem has a beautiful property: the solution depends only on the few points *touching* the margin — the **support vectors**. Everything else is irrelevant. That sparsity is why SVMs generalize well and are memory-efficient.

The dual form reveals that the optimizer only ever needs **inner products** `⟨xᵢ, xⱼ⟩` between data points — never the points themselves. This unlocks the kernel trick: replace `⟨xᵢ, xⱼ⟩` with a kernel `K(xᵢ, xⱼ)` that equals the inner product in some high-dimensional feature space `φ(x)`. You get the separating power of that huge (even infinite) space while *never computing φ*. An RBF kernel implicitly lifts to infinite dimensions for the price of one exponential.

Non-obvious insight: the kernel trick is not really about SVMs — it's a general recipe. *Any* algorithm expressible purely through inner products can be kernelized into a nonlinear one.
