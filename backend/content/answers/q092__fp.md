A time series is one observed signal, but the forces shaping it operate on *different timescales*, and they're tangled together. The central idea is **decomposition**: any series can be modeled as a sum (or product) of components, each capturing variation at its own frequency:

- **Trend** — the low-frequency, long-run direction. Is the level drifting up or down over the whole span?
- **Seasonality** — variation on a *fixed, known period* (daily, weekly, yearly). Retail spikes every December; traffic dips every weekend.
- **Cyclical** — longer, *irregular* swings with no fixed period (business cycles).
- **Residual / noise** — what's left after removing the structured parts; the irreducible randomness.

Why must we separate them? Because they answer different questions and demand different actions. **Trend vs. seasonality is fundamentally a frequency distinction**: trend is the slow envelope; seasonality is the fast, *repeating* oscillation around it. Confusing them causes classic errors—declaring "sales are up!" in December when it's just the annual bump, or missing real decline hidden under a seasonal peak.

This is essentially **spectral thinking**: you're decomposing a signal into components by frequency so each can be modeled and forecast on its own terms.

The non-obvious insight: seasonality is defined by *knowing the period in advance*. The instant a repeating pattern's length becomes unpredictable, it's no longer seasonality but a cycle—and the entire modeling approach must change.
