A random forest is an ensemble of decision trees combined by **bagging** plus feature randomness, and it exists to fix the one fatal flaw of a single tree: variance. A deep decision tree overfits badly — shuffle the data slightly and you get a completely different tree. Averaging many trees cancels that noise.

Two sources of randomness make the trees **decorrelated**, which is the whole trick. First, each tree trains on a **bootstrap sample** (sampled with replacement). Second — and this is what separates a random forest from plain bagged trees — at *each split* only a random subset of features is considered. Without that, one dominant feature would headline every tree and they'd all look alike, so averaging wouldn't help. Predictions combine by majority vote (classification) or averaging (regression).

Why it beats one tree: averaging many *decorrelated* high-variance models slashes variance without adding much bias, so you get a much more stable model that generalizes. Bonus gifts: **out-of-bag error** (a free validation estimate from the ~37% of rows each tree didn't see), and **feature importances**.

Where I actually reach for it: it's my strong, low-effort baseline on tabular data — robust to outliers, needs no feature scaling, hard to overfit, and forgiving of hyperparameters. The honest caveats: you lose the single tree's interpretability, and on raw accuracy a well-tuned **gradient-boosting** model (XGBoost/LightGBM) usually edges it out. Random forest for a quick robust baseline; boosting when I'm squeezing for the last points.
