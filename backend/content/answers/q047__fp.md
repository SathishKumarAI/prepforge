The problem: a deep network has millions of weights, and to do gradient descent you need `∂L/∂w` for *every* one. Computing each derivative independently by perturbing a weight would cost one forward pass per weight — hopelessly expensive. Backprop computes them **all at once, in a single backward pass**, for essentially the cost of one forward pass.

The engine is the chain rule plus one crucial observation: a network is a *composition* of functions, `L = f_n(...f_2(f_1(x)))`. The derivative of a composition is a product of local derivatives. Different weights share long prefixes of that product. So instead of recomputing shared factors, you compute them once and *reuse* them — this is dynamic programming applied to the chain rule.

Concretely: the forward pass caches each layer's activations. The backward pass carries a single quantity, the "error signal" `δ = ∂L/∂(layer input)`, from output toward input. At each layer, `δ` is multiplied by the local Jacobian to get the previous layer's `δ`, and combined with cached activations to yield that layer's weight gradients. Reverse-mode automatic differentiation, exactly.

Non-obvious insight: backprop isn't special to neural nets — it's reverse-mode autodiff, optimal whenever you have *many inputs and few outputs* (here, millions of weights, one scalar loss). That asymmetry is precisely why we differentiate backward, not forward.
