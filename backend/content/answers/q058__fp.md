The normal (Gaussian) is the bell-shaped distribution defined by just two numbers, mean and variance, with density `∝ exp(−(x−μ)²/2σ²)`. But *why* this particular curve dominates statistics isn't obvious from the formula — it follows from three deep principles that all point to the same shape.

First, the **Central Limit Theorem**: sums (and averages) of many independent influences converge to a normal, *regardless of the components' own distributions*. Since most measured quantities are aggregates of many small random effects — height, measurement error, test scores — the normal appears everywhere not by coincidence but by mathematical inevitability. It's the attractor of averaging.

Second, **maximum entropy**: among all distributions with a given mean and variance, the normal is the one that assumes the *least* — maximum uncertainty subject to those two constraints. So if all you honestly know is a center and a spread, the normal is the least presumptuous choice.

Third, **analytical convenience**: it's closed under addition and linear transforms, and it's the conjugate/likelihood that makes least squares, ANOVA, and much of inference tractable.

Non-obvious insight: its ubiquity is also a *trap*. Real data has fat tails — the exponential decay of the Gaussian makes extreme events look near-impossible, so assuming normality catastrophically underestimates rare risks (finance learned this in 2008). The distribution's mathematical convenience is exactly what makes over-applying it dangerous.
