The three paradigms are not arbitrary categories — they are the three possible answers to one question: *what feedback signal does the environment give you?*

**Supervised learning**: the environment hands you the correct answer for each input. You have pairs (x, y), and you learn the mapping by minimizing the gap between prediction and truth. Feedback is *dense and exact* — every example tells you precisely what you should have said. Geometrically, you're fitting a function to labeled points.

**Unsupervised learning**: the environment gives you inputs but *no answers at all*. So the only thing you can optimize toward is structure intrinsic to the data itself — density, compression, reconstruction. You minimize description length or maximize likelihood, discovering clusters and manifolds. Feedback is *self-supplied* by the geometry of the data.

**Reinforcement learning**: the environment gives feedback, but it's *sparse, delayed, and evaluative* — a scalar reward that says "that was good/bad" without saying what the right action was. You must learn from consequences, and credit-assign backward through time.

The non-obvious insight: information content decreases from supervised → RL. RL is hardest precisely because reward carries far fewer bits than a label — you must actively explore to generate your own informative data.
