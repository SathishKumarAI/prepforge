An error metric is a *value judgment about which mistakes hurt most*, encoded as a function of residuals. That framing explains all four.

- **MAE** = mean |error|. It weights every unit of error equally, so it estimates the **median** of the conditional distribution and is robust to outliers. Its geometry is the L1 norm — a diamond.
- **MSE** = mean error². Squaring means a 10-unit miss is penalized 100×, not 10× — it obsesses over large errors. This isn't arbitrary: minimizing squared error yields the **mean**, and it's the maximum-likelihood loss under Gaussian noise, which is *why* it dominates optimization (smooth, differentiable, convex). Units are squared, so it's hard to read.
- **RMSE** = √MSE. Just MSE returned to the original units for interpretability, while keeping the outlier sensitivity. RMSE ≥ MAE always, and the *gap* between them is itself a diagnostic of error-variance/outliers.
- **R²** = 1 − SS_res/SS_total. It rescales error against a baseline — "how much variance do I explain versus just predicting the mean?" This makes it unitless and comparable across problems; 0 means no better than the mean, negative means worse.

The insight most miss: choosing between MAE and RMSE is choosing your **loss geometry and implied noise model** — it decides whether a few big misses or many small ones define "good." And R² can be inflated simply by adding features (hence adjusted R²) and is misleading on non-linear or non-stationary data.
