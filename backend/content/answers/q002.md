question: "What is the Turing Test and what does it measure?"
topic: "AI"

The Turing Test, proposed by Alan Turing in 1950 as the "Imitation Game," measures whether a machine's conversational behavior is indistinguishable from a human's. A judge holds text-only conversations with a hidden human and a hidden machine; if the judge can't reliably tell which is which, the machine passes. Crucially, it measures *behavioral indistinguishability*, not understanding, consciousness, or reasoning.

The important nuance is what it deliberately ignores. Searle's Chinese Room argument makes the point sharply: a system can produce human-like output by symbol manipulation without any comprehension. So passing tells you the outputs are convincing, not that anything is "thinking."

In practice the test has aged into a poor engineering benchmark. Modern LLMs routinely fool people in short exchanges, yet that says more about human priors and short context windows than about capability. What actually matters in production is task-grounded evaluation: exact-match/F1 on QA, pass@k on code, human preference win-rates, faithfulness and hallucination-rate on retrieval tasks. I've seen teams waste time chasing "human-like" chattiness when the deployed metric they needed was factual accuracy and calibration. The Turing Test is historically foundational and a great conceptual framing for "what counts as intelligence," but you don't ship against it — you ship against measurable, task-specific criteria.
