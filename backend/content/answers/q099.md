question: "In an analytics context, how do you know if a change in a metric is statistically significant or just noise?"
topic: "Data Analytics"

The core move is to **quantify how much the metric normally fluctuates, then check whether the observed change exceeds that natural variation** — you can't judge a jump without a sense of the baseline noise.

For a controlled comparison (an A/B test), this is a **hypothesis test**: state a null hypothesis (no difference), compute a test statistic and **p-value**, and reject if it's below your threshold (typically 0.05) — a two-proportion z-test for conversion rates, a t-test for continuous means. But the p-value alone isn't enough. I always insist on three things alongside it: a **confidence interval** on the *effect size* (a "significant" 0.01% lift may be worthless), adequate **statistical power** and a pre-computed **sample size**, and a check that the test ran its planned duration. The most common production sin is **peeking** — stopping the test the moment it crosses significance — which inflates false positives dramatically; use sequential testing or fix the horizon in advance.

For an **observed change in a live metric** with no control group (yesterday's signups jumped), significance testing is weaker because you can't isolate the cause. There I lean on **control charts** — is the point outside ±2–3 standard deviations of the historical mean? — or compare against the **seasonal/YoY baseline** rather than the prior period, since day-of-week and seasonality masquerade as signal. Always confront **base rates and multiple comparisons**: if you're watching fifty metrics, some will "spike" by chance every day. Significant ≠ meaningful ≠ causal — keep those three separate.
