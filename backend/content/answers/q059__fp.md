An outlier is not simply an extreme value — it's a point that is *improbable under the model you assume generated the data*. That framing is the key, because "improbable" only has meaning relative to an assumed distribution, and choosing that reference is the whole art. The same point can be an outlier under a Gaussian and perfectly ordinary under a heavy-tailed law.

Detection methods are just different assumed models plus a threshold on surprise:

- **Z-score** assumes normality: flag points more than ~3σ from the mean. Fatal flaw — mean and σ are *themselves* inflated by the outliers you're hunting (masking).
- **IQR / boxplot** replaces mean and σ with median and quartiles, which are robust; flag beyond `Q1 − 1.5·IQR` or `Q3 + 1.5·IQR`. Works on skewed data precisely because it doesn't trust the tails.
- **Multivariate** (Mahalanobis distance, isolation forests, LOF) catches points that are ordinary on each axis but jointly impossible — a 7-foot, 100-lb person.

Non-obvious insight: the real decision is *never* purely statistical. An outlier is either an **error** (delete/fix) or a **discovery** (the whole point — fraud, disease, a novel phenomenon). Ozone-hole readings were auto-discarded as outliers for years. So detect statistically, but *judge* with domain knowledge — deleting outliers by default can throw away the most valuable signal in the data.
