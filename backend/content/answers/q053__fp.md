Every model and test you will later run rests on *assumptions* — about distributions, relationships, independence, data quality. EDA exists because those assumptions are cheap to state and expensive to violate, and the only way to check them is to actually look at the data before committing to any model. Tukey framed it as *confirmatory analysis's* necessary predecessor: you must know what questions the data can answer before you start answering them.

Mechanically, EDA is systematic looking at three levels. **Univariate:** each variable's distribution — shape, center, spread, skew, outliers, missingness. **Bivariate:** how pairs relate — scatterplots, correlations, cross-tabs — surfacing signal and redundancy. **Multivariate:** interactions and structure across many variables at once.

Why lean so heavily on *visualization* rather than summary numbers? Because summaries compress, and compression hides. Anscombe's quartet and the Datasaurus make the point brutally: four datasets with identical means, variances, and correlations look wildly different when plotted. The human visual system detects clusters, gaps, curves, and anomalies that no single statistic reports.

Non-obvious insight: EDA's real product isn't charts — it's a *revised problem statement*. You routinely discover the target is mislabeled, a "feature" leaks the answer, or the population isn't who you thought. EDA is where you find out your original question was wrong, cheaply, before modeling makes the mistake expensive.
