Both answer "do two variables move together?" but they differ in *what kind of togetherness* they can even detect — and that difference is entirely about which mathematical structure you assume.

**Pearson** measures **linear** association: covariance normalized by the two standard deviations. It's literally the cosine of the angle between the mean-centered data vectors — a geometric projection. Because it lives on raw values, it assumes a straight-line relationship and is sensitive to outliers (one extreme point swings covariance hard).

**Spearman** is Pearson computed on the **ranks** rather than the raw values. Replacing values with ranks throws away all information except *order*. So Spearman detects any **monotonic** relationship — as X rises, Y consistently rises (or falls), whether linearly or not. Ranking also makes it robust: an outlier becomes just "the largest rank," bounded in influence.

The unifying principle: correlation is a projection, and the choice of Pearson vs. Spearman is a choice of *coordinate system* — the actual metric space vs. the ordinal space. You match it to what your data actually respects.

The insight most miss: **both being near zero does not mean independence.** A perfect U-shape (Y = X²) has Pearson ≈ 0 *and* Spearman ≈ 0, yet Y is fully determined by X. Correlation only captures monotone/linear structure; capturing arbitrary dependence needs tools like mutual information.
