The goal in an MDP is a policy maximizing long-term reward, but there's a chicken-and-egg problem: you can't evaluate a policy without knowing future values, and you can't compute values without a policy. Q-learning breaks this by learning a single object that encodes both: **Q(s,a)** — the expected total discounted reward of taking action a in state s and acting optimally thereafter. Given Q, the optimal policy is trivial: pick the action with the highest Q.

The engine is the Bellman optimality equation, which says Q(s,a) equals the immediate reward plus the discounted value of the *best* action in the next state: Q(s,a) = r + γ·max_a' Q(s',a'). Q-learning turns this identity into an update rule. After each real transition, it nudges the current estimate toward this target — the **temporal-difference error**, r + γ·max Q(s',a') − Q(s,a), measures how wrong the estimate was, and we correct by a step size α.

Why it works: this is **bootstrapping** — updating a guess from a better guess — combined with sampling real transitions, so no model of the environment is needed. Crucially, the `max` makes it **off-policy**: it learns the *optimal* value even while behaving exploratorily (e.g., ε-greedy).

The non-obvious insight: Q-learning learns the value of a policy it isn't following. That off-policy `max` is what lets it explore freely yet still converge to optimal behavior.
