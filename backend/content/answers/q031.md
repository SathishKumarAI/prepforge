k-NN is the laziest algorithm in the book, and that's a feature. There's no training phase — it just stores the data. At prediction time it computes the distance from the query point to every training point, keeps the `k` closest, and either votes (classification) or averages (regression) their labels.

The knobs are few but they bite. **k** controls the bias-variance trade-off: `k=1` fits every noisy point (high variance), large `k` smooths the boundary until it underfits. Pick it with cross-validation and use an odd number for binary voting to avoid ties. The **distance metric** matters too — Euclidean is default, but Manhattan or cosine can fit the domain better.

The non-negotiable is **feature scaling**. Distances are dominated by whatever feature has the largest range, so an unscaled "income in dollars" will completely drown "age in years." Standardize or normalize first — this is the single most common way I see k-NN quietly fail.

Its real weaknesses are the **curse of dimensionality** (in high dimensions everything is roughly equidistant, so "nearest" stops meaning anything) and **inference cost** — every prediction is O(n), painful on large datasets unless you use a KD-tree, ball tree, or approximate nearest-neighbor index like FAISS or HNSW. I mostly use it as a strong baseline and for similarity search rather than as a production classifier.
