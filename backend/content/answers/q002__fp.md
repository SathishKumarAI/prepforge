Turing's genius was dissolving an unanswerable question. "Can machines think?" is hopeless because "think" has no agreed operational meaning — you can argue forever about inner experience. So he replaced it with something *observable*: can a machine produce conversational behavior indistinguishable, to a human interrogator via text, from a human's?

The deep move is **behaviorism as a measurement strategy**. By routing everything through a text channel, Turing strips away appearance, voice, and embodiment, isolating the one faculty he cared about — the capacity to converse flexibly across arbitrary topics. What it measures is therefore *indistinguishability of output distributions*, not internal mechanism.

This is also its famous limitation. The test conflates "thinking" with "convincingly imitating a thinker" — Searle's Chinese Room objection: a system could pass by symbol manipulation with no understanding. And it rewards deception (mimicking human error, typing delays) rather than raw capability.

The non-obvious insight: modern LLMs arguably pass casual versions, yet this made the test *less* interesting, not more. It revealed that fluent language is more separable from general reasoning than Turing assumed. The test measures a proxy; passing it exposed how leaky the proxy always was.
