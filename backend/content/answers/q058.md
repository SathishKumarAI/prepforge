The normal (Gaussian) distribution is the symmetric, bell-shaped distribution defined entirely by two parameters: its mean μ (center) and standard deviation σ (spread). Its importance is out of proportion to how often data is *actually* normal, and understanding why is the real question.

The reason is the **Central Limit Theorem**: the sum or mean of many independent random variables tends toward a normal distribution regardless of the underlying distribution's shape. That's why *sampling distributions* of statistics are approximately normal even when the raw data isn't — which underpins confidence intervals, t-tests, and most classical inference. A/B test math works because the difference in conversion means is asymptotically normal, not because conversions are.

It's also mathematically convenient: it's the maximum-entropy distribution for a given mean and variance, it's closed under linear combinations, and it's the assumed error distribution in ordinary least squares (making OLS the maximum-likelihood estimator). The empirical rule — 68/95/99.7% within 1/2/3σ — makes it easy to reason about.

The trap is *assuming* normality. In production, financial returns, latencies, and counts are famously heavy-tailed or skewed; treating them as normal underestimates tail risk badly. Always check with a histogram or Q-Q plot (or a Shapiro-Wilk test on smaller samples) before leaning on normality, and remember the CLT is about the sampling distribution, not your raw feature.
