question: "What is the normal distribution and why is it important?"
topic: "Data Science"

Measure the height of thousands of adults and plot how many fall at each height. You'll get a **bell shape**: most people cluster near the average, with fewer and fewer as you move toward the very short or very tall extremes, symmetric on both sides. That's the **normal (Gaussian) distribution**, described by just two numbers — the **mean** (where the peak sits) and the **standard deviation** (how wide the bell spreads).

A handy rule: about **68%** of values land within one standard deviation of the mean, **95%** within two, and **99.7%** within three.

Why care? Tons of natural measurements roughly follow it. More importantly, the **Central Limit Theorem** says averages of samples tend toward this bell shape even when the raw data isn't — which is why so much of statistics (confidence intervals, hypothesis tests) leans on it. Just remember real data isn't always normal, so check with a histogram first.
