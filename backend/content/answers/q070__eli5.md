question: "Explain MAE, MSE, RMSE, and R-squared for regression evaluation."
topic: "Data Science"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"
sources: []

Imagine grading a weather forecaster on predicted temperatures. Each day there's a gap between the guess and reality, and these metrics summarize those gaps differently.

**MAE** averages the sizes of the misses. Simple, in real degrees, and it treats small and huge misses proportionally — outliers don't dominate.

**MSE** squares each miss before averaging, so one big blunder hurts far more. But the units become "degrees squared," which is hard to picture.

**RMSE** is the square root of MSE, back in normal degrees while still punishing big misses harder.

**R-squared** is different: a 0-to-1 score for how much of the temperature's ups and downs your model explains. 1 is perfect; 0 means no better than always guessing the average.

Use MAE when outliers shouldn't rule, RMSE when big misses are costly, and R-squared to tell stakeholders "we explain X% of the variation."
