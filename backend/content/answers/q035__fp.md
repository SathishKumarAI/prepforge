Imagine training your model on many different datasets drawn from the same source. Its predictions at a point will scatter. The expected squared error decomposes *exactly* into three pieces:

**Error = Bias² + Variance + Irreducible noise.**

- **Bias** — how far the *average* prediction sits from the truth. High bias = the model is too rigid to capture the real pattern (underfitting).
- **Variance** — how much predictions *wobble* across different training sets. High variance = the model is so flexible it chases the particular sample's noise (overfitting).
- **Irreducible noise** — inherent randomness no model can remove.

The "trade-off" is that bias and variance pull in opposite directions along the axis of **model capacity**. A constant predictor has zero variance but huge bias; a model that memorizes every point has near-zero bias but wild variance. Total error is U-shaped, so there's a *sweet spot* of complexity, and every regularization technique is really a knob to move along this curve.

The deep reason this trade-off exists: with finite data you're spending a fixed budget of "information." Fitting the signal more aggressively inevitably also fits noise — you can't tell them apart from inside the sample.

The non-obvious insight: the clean U-curve isn't universal. Massively over-parameterized models exhibit **double descent** — error rises, then *falls again* past the interpolation point — because implicit regularization (e.g., SGD favoring smooth solutions) tames variance despite enormous capacity.
