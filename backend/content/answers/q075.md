The core problem is **confounding**: in observational data, treated and untreated groups differ systematically, so a naive difference in outcomes mixes the treatment effect with those pre-existing differences. The whole toolkit is about approximating the randomization you couldn't run.

The methods, roughly by the assumption they lean on:

- **Adjustment / regression / propensity scores**: if you can *measure* the confounders, condition on them. Match or weight units by their propensity (modeled probability of treatment) so treated and control groups become comparable. Assumes **no unmeasured confounders** — the strong, unverifiable assumption. Draw a DAG first to decide what to adjust for (and crucially, what *not* to — never condition on a collider or a post-treatment variable).
- **Difference-in-Differences**: compare the before/after change in a treated group against a control group, differencing out fixed group differences. Relies on the **parallel-trends** assumption — plot pre-period trends to sanity-check it.
- **Instrumental variables**: find a variable that shifts treatment but affects the outcome *only through* treatment. Handles unmeasured confounding but good instruments are rare and hard to defend.
- **Regression discontinuity**: exploit a threshold rule (a score cutoff for a program) — units just above and below are quasi-randomized.

In production I treat every observational "effect" as a hypothesis, not a conclusion. State the assumptions explicitly, run sensitivity analyses (how strong would an unmeasured confounder have to be to overturn this?), and where possible validate against an eventual experiment. Tools like DoWhy help by forcing you to declare the causal graph and refute it.
