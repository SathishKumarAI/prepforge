**Cohort analysis** groups users by a shared starting characteristic — most commonly their signup or first-purchase period — and tracks each group's behavior over time. Instead of asking "what's our retention?", you ask "of users who joined in January, what fraction were still active in month 1, 2, 3…?" and compare that curve to the February cohort, the March cohort, and so on.

Why it's useful: **aggregate metrics hide lifecycle and mix effects.** Your overall active-user count can look flat and healthy while every individual cohort is churning faster than the last — new signups are just masking the decay. Cohorts unmask that. They also let you measure whether a product change actually improved retention: if the post-launch cohorts bend upward relative to earlier ones, you have evidence the change worked, controlled for tenure.

The classic artifact is a **retention triangle** — cohorts as rows, periods-since-start as columns, retention % in the cells. Reading down a column shows whether newer cohorts are getting better or worse; reading across a row shows a cohort's natural decay and whether it flattens (a healthy retention plateau) or trends to zero.

Cohorts aren't only time-based. Segmenting by acquisition channel, plan tier, or first feature used often reveals that "paid search users retain at half the rate of organic," which redirects spend. In production the biggest gotcha is defining "active" consistently and handling users with no activity in a period correctly — an off-by-one there quietly inflates every retention number.
