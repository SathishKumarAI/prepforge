question: "What is the difference between mean, median, and mode, and when is each useful?"
topic: "Data Science"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"
sources: []

Picture a room of ten ordinary workers, and you want one number for the "typical" salary. Then a billionaire walks in. The **mean** (add everything up, divide by the count) suddenly jumps to millions — technically correct, but it describes nobody in the room, because a single extreme value drags it. That's the mean's weakness: it's **sensitive to outliers**.

The **median** — literally the middle value once you line everyone up — barely budges, because it only cares who's in the middle, not how rich the richest is. That's why it's preferred for skewed things like income or house prices.

The **mode** is simply the most common value, and it's the only one that works for categories, like the most-purchased product color.

Quick rule: use the mean for tidy, symmetric numbers; the median when outliers or skew are present; and the mode for categorical data or spotting the most frequent value.
