Overfitting is when a model memorizes the training data — including its noise and quirks — instead of learning the underlying pattern, so it scores great on training data and poorly on anything new. It's the signature of **high variance**. The diagnostic is dead simple: a large gap between training and validation performance. Training accuracy 0.99, validation 0.72 — that's overfitting, full stop.

The fixes, roughly in the order I try them:

- **More data** — the most reliable cure when you can get it; augmentation (crops, flips, paraphrases) is a cheap proxy.
- **Regularization** — L1/L2 penalties, and **dropout** for neural nets.
- **Simpler model / capacity control** — fewer parameters, shallower trees; for boosting, lower depth and learning rate.
- **Early stopping** — halt when validation loss stops improving; my go-to for gradient-boosted trees and deep nets.
- **Cross-validation** — so you're not fooled by one lucky split while tuning.
- **Ensembling** (bagging) to average out variance.

The lived experience I'd share: overfitting rarely shows up as a dramatic training curve. It shows up months later as a model that crushed offline metrics and quietly underperforms in production — and nine times out of ten the real culprit was **data leakage** inflating the validation score, not model capacity. So before I reach for heavier regularization, I audit the split and the features. The mirror-image failure is **underfitting** (too simple to capture the signal); the goal is the bias–variance sweet spot, not zero training error.
