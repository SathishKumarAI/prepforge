1. **Clarify first.** Decide whether they want the historical definition or a critique of its validity — the safest move is to give both. Assume they want you to separate *what it tests* from *what it does not*.
2. **Mental model.** Frame it as the "Imitation Game": a hidden human vs. a hidden machine, one human judge, text-only conversation. The metric is the judge's inability to tell them apart.
3. **Reason step by step.** Attribute it to Turing (1950). Explain the setup, then pin the key insight: it measures **behavioral indistinguishability** in natural language, not internal understanding, consciousness, or reasoning quality.
4. **Traps to avoid.** Don't claim it measures "intelligence" flatly, and don't say passing proves understanding — cite **Searle's Chinese Room** as the counterargument. Also avoid saying no machine has passed; note modern LLMs often fool judges in short chats, which is exactly why the test's usefulness is debated.
5. **Sanity-check + delivery.** Ask yourself: does my definition survive the Chinese Room objection? It should, because I framed it as behavioral, not cognitive. Deliver in one line: "It's a test of whether a machine's conversation is indistinguishable from a human's — a behavioral proxy, not proof of real thought."
