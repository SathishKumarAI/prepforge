question: "What is the difference between a star schema and a snowflake schema?"
topic: "Data Analytics"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"

---

Both schemas solve one analytical problem: separate the *measurements* you sum from the *context* you slice by. A central **fact table** holds numeric events (sales, clicks) plus foreign keys; surrounding **dimension tables** hold descriptive context (product, date, store). The only question is: *how far do you normalize the dimensions?*

A **star schema** keeps each dimension flat—one denormalized table per dimension, even if that means repeating "Electronics" a million times in the product dimension. A **snowflake schema** normalizes dimensions into sub-tables: product → category → department, linked by keys, forming branching "snowflake" arms.

The choice is a pure instance of the **normalization trade-off**, but resolved differently than in OLTP. Snowflaking saves storage and guarantees no update anomalies, but every query must traverse more joins. Star schemas duplicate data (cheap—dimensions are small relative to facts) to *minimize joins*, which is what analytical query engines optimize for.

The deeper principle: in a warehouse, **read-throughput dominates the cost function**, and dimension redundancy is nearly free because the fact table dwarfs the dimensions. So the OLTP instinct to normalize *inverts* here.

The non-obvious insight most miss: snowflaking's real payoff isn't storage—it's when a dimension is genuinely *hierarchical and shared* (a conformed geography), where one authoritative sub-table prevents inconsistent rollups. Otherwise, star wins for its simplicity and speed.
