question: "What is bootstrapping and when is it useful?"

The uncertainty of any estimate (a median, a correlation, an AUC) comes from the fact that your sample is one random draw; a different sample would give a different number. To quantify that, you'd love to re-run the experiment thousands of times and watch the estimate vary — but you only have one dataset. Bootstrapping is the audacious workaround: **treat your sample as if it were the population, and resample from it.**

The mechanism: draw new datasets of the same size *with replacement* from your data, recompute the statistic on each, and the spread of those thousands of values approximates the sampling distribution. Standard errors, confidence intervals, and bias all fall out empirically.

Why it works is a real principle: the empirical distribution (equal mass on each observed point) is the nonparametric MLE of the true distribution. Sampling from it mimics sampling from nature — the "plug-in principle." With-replacement is essential: it's what injects the variation; without it you'd just recover the original sample every time.

When it shines: statistics with no clean closed-form standard error (median, ratios, correlations, model metrics), small samples, or when normality assumptions fail. It trades human derivation for compute.

The insight most miss: bootstrapping cannot create information the sample lacks. It faithfully propagates *sampling* uncertainty but is blind to bias — a skewed or unrepresentative sample yields confident, tight, and wrong intervals.
