Consider how a flat table stores facts: rows and columns, where relationships are implicit in foreign keys and joins. This is fine for one schema but brittle when knowledge is heterogeneous, sparse, and richly interlinked — the real world doesn't fit a fixed set of columns. A **knowledge graph** answers this by making the *relationship itself* the first-class citizen.

The atomic unit is a **triple**: (subject, predicate, object) — "Einstein — bornIn — Ulm." Entities are nodes; relationships are labeled, directed edges. Knowledge becomes a graph, not a table. Why must this help? Because it turns reasoning into *graph traversal*: multi-hop questions ("physicists born in cities on the Danube") become path-following, and new schema is just new edge types — no migration. The structure is self-describing.

The deeper principle is **compositionality**: complex facts are built from a small vocabulary of relations, and this explicit structure supports *inference* — if A partOf B and B partOf C, transitivity yields A partOf C. Facts you never stored can be derived.

The non-obvious insight: a knowledge graph's power is less about storage than about making relationships *queryable and inferable* rather than buried in prose. It's the substrate that lets symbolic systems, search engines, and even LLM grounding reason over entities instead of merely matching strings.
