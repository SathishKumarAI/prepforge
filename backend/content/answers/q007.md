A* is a best-first search that finds the least-cost path by ranking nodes on `f(n) = g(n) + h(n)`, where `g(n)` is the known cost from the start to `n` and `h(n)` is the heuristic estimate of the cost remaining to the goal. It's the sweet spot between Dijkstra (uses only `g`, guaranteed optimal but explores blindly) and greedy best-first (uses only `h`, fast but not optimal).

Mechanically: keep a priority queue (the open set) keyed on `f`, plus a way to record the best `g` found per node. Pop the lowest-`f` node, and if it's the goal you're done. Otherwise expand its neighbors, compute a tentative `g`, and if that beats the neighbor's recorded cost, update its `g`, `f`, and parent pointer and push it. Repeat until the goal is popped.

Guarantees hinge on the heuristic: with an **admissible** `h` (never overestimates) A* is optimal; with a **consistent** `h` it never needs to re-expand a settled node. Set `h(n)=0` and it degenerates exactly to Dijkstra.

The real-world catch is memory — A* stores the whole frontier, `O(b^d)`, which blows up on large maps. In production I've reached for weighted A* (inflate `h` by a factor >1 for a big speedup at the cost of bounded suboptimality) or IDA*/jump-point search when RAM is the constraint. A good heuristic and a decent tie-breaking rule on equal `f` matter more than micro-optimizing the queue.
