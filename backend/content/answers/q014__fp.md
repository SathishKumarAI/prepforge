question: "What is Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)?"
topic: "AI"

An LLM stores knowledge in its weights — a *lossy, frozen* compression of its training data. This creates three structural problems: it can't know anything after training, it can't access your private documents, and it blurs details it half-remembers (hallucination). Retraining to fix any of these is absurdly expensive. **RAG** solves it by separating *parametric* knowledge (in the weights) from *non-parametric* knowledge (in an external store you can update freely).

The mechanism: index your documents as embedding vectors capturing meaning. At query time, embed the question, find the semantically nearest passages by vector similarity, and *inject them into the prompt* as context. The model then generates its answer conditioned on retrieved evidence rather than on memory alone. You've converted a closed-book exam into an open-book one.

Why this works: transformers are excellent at *using* information present in their context, even if they never memorized it. RAG plays to that strength — grounding — while sidestepping their weakness — recall.

The non-obvious insight: RAG shifts the hard problem from *generation* to *retrieval*. The model can only be as good as the passages you fetch, so system quality is dominated by chunking, embedding quality, and ranking — not the LLM. Garbage retrieved is garbage grounded, often stated with more confidence than a pure hallucination.
