Prompt engineering is designing the input to an LLM — instructions, context, examples, and output format — to reliably elicit the behavior you want, without touching the weights. It matters because LLM outputs are highly sensitive to phrasing and framing: the same underlying model can go from unusable to production-grade on a task purely through better prompting, and it's the cheapest, fastest adaptation lever you have before you spend money on RAG or fine-tuning.

The techniques that actually move the needle:

- **Explicit instructions and output schema** — specify format (JSON, fields) and constraints rather than hoping.
- **Few-shot examples** — demonstrations anchor tone, format, and edge-case handling far better than description.
- **Chain-of-thought** — "reason step by step" measurably lifts multi-step reasoning accuracy.
- **Delimiters and role framing** — separate instructions from untrusted data, which also blunts prompt injection.

Why it's not just fiddling: it's your first line of defense on reliability and safety. Structured prompts reduce hallucination, "say I don't know if the context doesn't contain it" reduces confident fabrication, and clean instruction/data separation reduces injection risk.

In production I treat prompts like code: version them, keep a regression eval set, and change one thing at a time — because prompts are brittle across model versions. A prompt tuned on one model silently degrades when the provider ships an update, so the real discipline is continuous evaluation, not clever wording.
