question: "Why does correlation not imply causation?"

Correlation just says two variables move together; it's silent on *why*. And there are at least four non-causal reasons they might:

- **Confounding** — a hidden third variable drives both. Ice-cream sales correlate with drownings; the confounder is hot weather. This is the big one in observational data.
- **Reverse causation** — you've got the arrow backwards. Hospitals correlate with deaths, but hospitals don't cause death.
- **Coincidence / spurious correlation** — with enough variables or small samples, things line up by chance. Test 100 features against an outcome and a few will "correlate" at p<0.05 by luck alone.
- **Selection bias** — how the data was gathered creates the association.

To actually claim causation you need to rule these out. The gold standard is a **randomized controlled experiment** (an A/B test): randomization balances confounders — known *and* unknown — across groups, so a difference in outcome is attributable to the treatment. When you can't randomize (ethics, cost, feasibility), you fall back on **causal inference** — instrumental variables, difference-in-differences, propensity matching, regression discontinuity — each leaning on assumptions you have to defend.

Why this matters on the job, not just as trivia: acting on a correlation as if it were causal burns money. "Users who use feature X retain better" does *not* mean forcing X on everyone lifts retention — heavy users self-select into X (confounding). I've seen roadmaps built on exactly that error. The fix is boringly reliable: run the experiment.
