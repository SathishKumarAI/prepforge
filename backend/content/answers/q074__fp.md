A single hypothesis test at α=0.05 accepts a 5% chance of a false positive. That's a deliberate, tolerable risk *for one test*. But false-positive risk **compounds across tests**: run many, and the probability that *at least one* fires by chance grows fast. This is pure probability — if each test independently avoids a false alarm with probability 0.95, then across m tests the chance of *no* false alarm is 0.95^m, so the family-wise error rate is 1 − 0.95^m. At m=20 that's ~64%. You are almost guaranteed a "significant" fluke.

This is why "p-hacking" and "torturing the data" work: test enough subgroups, metrics, or model variants and something crosses 0.05 by luck alone. The threshold was never designed to survive repetition.

Corrections restore honesty by making each test pass a stricter bar:

- **Bonferroni:** use α/m per test. Controls the family-wise error rate exactly but is conservative — it crushes power when m is large.
- **Benjamini–Hochberg (FDR):** don't demand *zero* false positives; instead control the *proportion* of discoveries that are false. Far more powerful, ideal when you expect many true effects (genomics, feature screening).

The insight most miss: the correction you need depends on the question — controlling "any false positive at all" (FWER) versus "an acceptable fraction of my hits are wrong" (FDR) are different goals, and the count m must include *every* test you ran, including the ones you didn't report.
