Both measure **spread** — how far the data typically sits from its mean. Variance is the average of the squared deviations from the mean; standard deviation is its square root. Squaring in variance keeps the math well-behaved (differentiable, additive for independent variables) but leaves you in squared units — dollars², seconds² — which are meaningless to interpret. Taking the square root gives you standard deviation, which is back in the original units, so it's what you actually report.

A detail that trips people up: the divisor. Population variance divides by *N*; sample variance divides by *N−1* (Bessel's correction) to get an unbiased estimate, because using the sample mean instead of the true mean slightly underestimates spread. `numpy.var` defaults to *N* (`ddof=0`); `pandas.Series.var` defaults to *N−1* (`ddof=1`). In production I've seen two "variance" numbers disagree in a report for exactly this reason — always check `ddof`.

Interpretively, low SD means values cluster tightly around the mean; high SD means they're diffuse. For roughly normal data the empirical rule (68/95/99.7) makes SD a ruler: ~95% of values fall within two SDs. But SD is itself a mean-based, outlier-sensitive statistic — for heavy-tailed or skewed data the IQR is a more honest spread measure, and SD can be badly inflated by a few extreme points.
