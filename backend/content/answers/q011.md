An LLM is a transformer trained to do one thing: predict the next **token** given all previous tokens. Generation is autoregressive — it produces text one token at a time, feeding each output back in as input.

The loop: your prompt is split into tokens (subword units, via BPE or similar), each mapped to an embedding. Those flow through stacked self-attention and feed-forward layers, and the final layer emits **logits** — a score for every token in the vocabulary. Apply softmax to get a probability distribution, sample one token (greedy, top-k, top-p, temperature), append it, and repeat until an end-of-sequence token or length limit.

Two things I always emphasize because they explain real failures. First, the model has **no database** — knowledge is baked into weights during pretraining, which is why it hallucinates confidently and why RAG exists to inject fresh facts. Second, generation cost is dominated by this token-by-token loop; that's why we cache the attention **KV state** so each new token doesn't recompute the whole sequence, and it's why latency scales with output length.

Pretraining (self-supervised next-token prediction on a huge corpus) gives raw capability; post-training — supervised fine-tuning plus preference optimization like RLHF or DPO — is what makes it follow instructions instead of just autocompleting. The base model and the assistant you talk to are the same architecture with very different post-training.
