RLHF is how a raw next-token predictor becomes a helpful assistant. A pretrained model can complete text but doesn't inherently follow instructions or refuse harmful requests; RLHF aligns it to human preferences in three stages.

**1. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT):** fine-tune the base model on high-quality demonstrations of good responses — teaching it the *format* of being an assistant. **2. Reward model:** show humans several outputs for the same prompt and have them rank them; train a separate model to predict those preferences as a scalar reward. This is clever because ranking is far easier and more consistent for annotators than writing ideal answers. **3. RL optimization:** treat the LLM as a policy and optimize it to maximize the reward model's score, classically with **PPO**, plus a **KL-divergence penalty** anchoring it to the SFT model. That penalty is essential — without it the policy drifts into gibberish that games the reward, the canonical **reward-hacking** failure.

The honest limitations: it's expensive (human labeling), the reward model inherits annotator biases and can be exploited, and PplusRL is finicky to stabilize. That's why **DPO** has largely displaced the PPO loop in practice — it reframes preference learning as a single classification-style loss directly on preferred/rejected pairs, skipping the explicit reward model and RL entirely. Same goal, far less operational pain, which is why it's become the default in many open-source alignment pipelines.
