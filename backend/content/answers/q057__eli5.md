question: "What do variance and standard deviation measure?"
topic: "Data Science"

Imagine two dart players who both *average* the bullseye. One lands every dart in a tight cluster; the other scatters them all over the board. Their averages are identical, yet they're clearly different players. **Variance** and **standard deviation** capture that difference — they measure how **spread out** the data is around its average.

To get variance, you take each point's distance from the mean, **square it** (so overshoots and undershoots both count as positive), and average those. The **standard deviation** is just the square root of that.

Why two measures? Variance comes out in awkward *squared* units, while standard deviation is back in the **original units**, so it's far more intuitive — it tells you a typical distance from the average.

For a bell-shaped distribution, about 68% of values fall within one standard deviation of the mean, and 95% within two. Both get thrown off by outliers, since distances are squared.
