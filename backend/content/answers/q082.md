Start from the **question and the data types**, not from the chart catalog. The message you're trying to convey — comparison, trend, distribution, composition, or relationship — dictates the encoding.

A quick working map:

- **Comparison across categories** → bar chart (horizontal if labels are long). Bars beat pie charts almost always because humans compare lengths far better than angles.
- **Trend over time** → line chart. Continuous time on the x-axis.
- **Distribution of one variable** → histogram or box plot.
- **Relationship between two variables** → scatter plot; add a trend line if you're claiming correlation.
- **Part-to-whole** → stacked bar or a treemap; reserve pie for two or three slices at most.
- **A single number** → just show the number big, with a comparison. A KPI tile beats a gauge.

The deeper principle is honoring perception: position and length are read most accurately, then color-hue and area least. So encode your most important variable with position/length.

The mistakes I see constantly in production dashboards: dual y-axes that invite spurious correlations, truncated y-axes that exaggerate tiny changes, pie charts with twelve slices, and 3D anything. Also match to audience — an executive wants one clear takeaway per chart; an analyst can handle a dense scatter or small multiples. When in doubt, a well-labeled bar or line chart is rarely wrong.
