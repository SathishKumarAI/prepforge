An outlier is an observation that sits far from the bulk of the data. The critical distinction, before any detection method, is *why* it's there: a data-entry error (age = 999), a legitimate rare event (a genuine whale customer), or a sign your distributional assumption is wrong. Those demand opposite responses — you delete the first, keep and often segment the second, and rethink your model for the third.

Detection methods, roughly in order of sophistication:

- **IQR rule**: flag points below Q1 − 1.5·IQR or above Q3 + 1.5·IQR. Robust, distribution-free, great default — it's what boxplots use.
- **Z-score**: flag |z| > 3. Simple but assumes normality and is itself corrupted by the outliers (the mean and SD get dragged), so it under-detects in heavy-tailed data. A robust z-score using median and MAD fixes that.
- **Multivariate / model-based**: Isolation Forest, Local Outlier Factor, or Mahalanobis distance catch points that are normal on each axis but anomalous jointly — a 20-year-old with a $2M mortgage.

In production I never auto-drop outliers. I flag, investigate a sample, and decide per-cause. Blindly capping or removing them is how you delete your most valuable customers or mask the fraud you were hired to catch. Winsorizing or a log transform is often better than deletion, and tree-based models are naturally fairly robust to them.
