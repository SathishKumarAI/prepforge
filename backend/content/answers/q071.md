Bayes' theorem is the rule for **updating a belief when new evidence arrives**:

`P(A | B) = [ P(B | A) · P(A) ] / P(B)`

`P(A)` is your **prior** (belief before evidence), `P(B|A)` the **likelihood** (how expected the evidence is if A holds), and `P(A|B)` the **posterior** (updated belief). `P(B)` normalizes.

The example that makes it visceral is the rare-disease test. Disease prevalence is 1% (prior = 0.01). The test is 99% sensitive and has a 5% false-positive rate. You test positive — how worried should you be?

`P(sick | +) = (0.99 · 0.01) / (0.99 · 0.01 + 0.05 · 0.99) ≈ 0.167`

About **17%**, not 99%. Almost everyone's intuition screams 99% and is wrong. The reason is the **base rate**: sick people are so rare that the small false-positive rate applied to the huge healthy population still generates more false positives than true positives. The prior dominates.

Why this matters beyond the puzzle: **base-rate neglect** is a pervasive real-world error. It's why a "99% accurate" fraud or cancer screen produces mostly false alarms when the true event is rare — and why you then run a *second, independent* test, which multiplies the evidence and sharpens the posterior. This same update mechanic underpins spam filters (naive Bayes), Bayesian A/B testing, and probabilistic ML generally. The one-line takeaway I'd leave an interviewer with: evidence updates a prior, it doesn't replace it.
