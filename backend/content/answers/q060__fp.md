question: "What is the difference between a population and a sample, and why do we sample?"

The population is the *entire* set of units you actually care about — every voter, every transaction, every possible measurement. A sample is a subset you observe. The distinction matters because your goal is a statement about the population, but your evidence is only the sample: all of inference is the discipline of controlling that gap.

Why not just measure the population? Sometimes it's impossible (the population is infinite or hypothetical — "all future coin flips"), often it's destructive (you can't crash-test every car) or simply too costly and slow. But the profound reason is **diminishing returns**. Estimation error shrinks like `1/√n`, *independent of population size*. To halve your error you quadruple `n` — and once `n` is large, adding more buys almost nothing. A well-drawn sample of 1,000 estimates a national proportion nearly as precisely as a sample of 10,000, whether the country has 1 million or 1 billion people.

The catch is that the `1/√n` guarantee assumes a **random** sample — every unit equally likely. Bias, not size, is the real enemy: the 1936 *Literary Digest* poll had 2.4 million responses and still called the election wrong, because its sample was systematically skewed.

Non-obvious insight: for accuracy, **representativeness beats volume**. A small random sample crushes a huge biased one, because more data reduces variance but does nothing to remove bias.
