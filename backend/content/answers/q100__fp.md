Reframe the goal from first principles: analysis has *zero value* until it changes a decision. Communication is therefore not a postscript—it's the **conversion step of the entire analytical pipeline**, where information becomes action. The bottleneck is not your model's rigor; it's the **cognitive and incentive gap** between what you know and what the decision-maker can absorb and act on.

Two constraints govern everything. First, the audience's **attention and working memory are tiny**, so structure must front-load the conclusion. Lead with the *so-what*—the decision and its expected impact—*then* the supporting evidence. This is the inverted pyramid, and it works because it lets a busy stakeholder act correctly even if they stop reading after the first sentence.

Second, humans reason with **narratives and comparisons, not raw statistics**. "$2M at risk" lands; "churn coefficient 0.037" doesn't. Translate every finding into the stakeholder's own metric of value (revenue, cost, risk, time) and anchor it against a reference point so its magnitude is *felt*.

Then make action frictionless: pair each finding with a **specific, owned recommendation**, and be honest about uncertainty so trust compounds across future reports.

The non-obvious insight: your job is **decision compression, not information transfer.** Every detail you include competes for attention with the one thing they must do. Ruthlessly cutting true-but-irrelevant findings *increases* impact—clarity is subtraction, and the recommendation, not the analysis, is the deliverable.
