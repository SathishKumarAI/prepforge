question: "How does a decision tree decide where to split?"
topic: "Machine Learning"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"

---

A **decision tree** is like the game of Twenty Questions: it keeps asking yes/no questions to narrow things down. At each step it must choose the *best* question — and "best" means the one that most cleanly **splits the group into purer piles**. Imagine a bin of mixed red and blue marbles. A good question ("is the marble bigger than 2cm?") is one where, afterward, one bin is mostly red and the other mostly blue. The tree tries every feature and threshold, scores how pure the resulting groups are using measures like **Gini impurity** or **entropy** for categories (or variance reduction for numbers), then picks the winner. It repeats down each branch until the piles are pure or it hits a stopping limit. Trees are wonderfully readable, but grown too deep they memorize noise — so we prune them or combine many into forests.
