question: "Why does correlation not imply causation?"
topic: "Data Science"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"

---

Correlation is a statement about a *joint distribution you passively observed*: X and Y move together. Causation is a statement about *interventions*: if I reach in and change X, Y changes. These are fundamentally different objects, and observational data only ever hands you the first.

The reason one can't imply the other is that a correlation between X and Y is consistent with several incompatible causal worlds: X→Y, Y→X, a common cause Z→X and Z→Y (confounding), or even selection on a common effect (collider bias). All produce the *same* correlation. Observation alone cannot distinguish them, because it never breaks the links — you see the system running, not the system perturbed.

This is why the *randomized* experiment is the gold standard: randomization severs every incoming arrow to X, so any surviving association with Y must be causal. Absent randomization, a confounder Z can manufacture correlation with zero causal connection (ice-cream sales and drownings, both driven by summer heat).

Formally, `P(Y | X = x)` — conditioning, what you compute from data — is not `P(Y | do(X = x))` — intervening, what causation means. They coincide only when there is no confounding.

Non-obvious insight: causation isn't a *stronger* correlation you get with more data. No amount of purely observational data closes the gap; you need an intervention or a defensible causal assumption.
