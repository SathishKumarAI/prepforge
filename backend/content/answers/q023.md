They're two opposing paradigms for how a machine represents and manipulates knowledge. **Symbolic AI** (GOFAI) encodes knowledge explicitly as symbols, logic, and human-readable rules — expert systems, logic programming, planners, knowledge graphs. It's top-down and rule-driven. **Connectionist AI** learns distributed, sub-symbolic representations from data by adjusting weights in neural networks. It's bottom-up and data-driven.

The trade-offs are almost mirror images. Symbolic systems are interpretable, verifiable, and strong at explicit reasoning, planning, and enforcing hard constraints — but brittle, labor-intensive to build (manual knowledge engineering), and helpless with noisy or perceptual input. Neural systems excel at perception (vision, speech, language) and generalization from examples, degrade gracefully on messy data, but are black boxes, data- and compute-hungry, and historically weak at multi-step logical reasoning and guaranteeing constraints.

The reason this question still matters is **neuro-symbolic AI**, which is where a lot of real systems now live: neural perception feeding symbolic reasoning, or symbolic structure guiding neural learning. In production I've reached for exactly this hybrid — an LLM (connectionist) does the fuzzy natural-language understanding, then calls out to a deterministic rules engine or a solver for the parts that must be correct and auditable (pricing, eligibility, compliance). You don't want a neural net *inferring* a tax rule; you want it to *route* to the rule. The pragmatic lesson from twelve years of shipping: use neural nets for perception and language, symbolic logic for anything that has to be exact and explainable.
