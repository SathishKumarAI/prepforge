question: "What is a confidence interval and how should it be interpreted?"

A confidence interval is a range, computed from your sample, that's likely to contain the true **population parameter** at a stated confidence level. For a mean it's roughly `estimate ± (critical value) × standard error`, e.g. `x̄ ± 1.96·(s/√n)` for a 95% CI on a large sample.

The interpretation trips up almost everyone, so I'm precise about it. **Correct:** "If I repeated this sampling procedure many times and built a CI each time, about 95% of those intervals would contain the true value." The confidence is a property of the *procedure*, not of any one interval. **Wrong (but tempting):** "There's a 95% probability the true value is in *this* interval." In frequentist terms the parameter is a fixed number — it's either in this specific interval or it isn't; the randomness lives in the sampling, not the parameter. (If you want the "95% probability it's in this range" statement, that's a Bayesian *credible* interval, which requires a prior.)

Why I prefer reporting a CI over a bare p-value: it carries **both** significance and magnitude. A 95% CI for a treatment effect of `[-0.1%, +0.2%]` tells you it's not significant (it straddles zero) *and* that even the optimistic end is tiny — so you shouldn't ship. A p-value alone hides that. Width encodes uncertainty: intervals narrow with **larger samples** and **lower variability** (again the `1/√n` from standard error). Practically, I always translate the CI into decision language for stakeholders — "the effect is somewhere between trivial and small" — rather than leaving them a naked number.
