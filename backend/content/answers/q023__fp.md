question: "What is the difference between symbolic AI and connectionist (neural) AI?"

The split traces to a single question: **where does knowledge live?**

Symbolic AI assumes intelligence is the manipulation of discrete, human-readable symbols by explicit rules — logic, search, knowledge bases. Knowledge lives in *statements we write down*. Its strength follows directly: because reasoning is explicit, it composes, generalizes to novel combinations, and is interpretable and verifiable. Its fatal weakness is the same explicitness — someone must hand-author the symbols and rules, which is brittle and can't cope with the perceptual mess of raw pixels or the ambiguity of language (the "knowledge acquisition bottleneck").

Connectionist AI assumes knowledge lives in the *weights of a distributed network*, learned by adjusting connections to fit data. Meaning isn't in any single unit but in patterns across many — hence robustness to noise and graceful degradation. It excels exactly where symbolic AI fails: perception and pattern-matching from data. Its weakness mirrors symbolic AI's strength — it's opaque, data-hungry, and struggles with systematic compositional reasoning.

The deeper principle: this is a trade-off between **built-in structure vs. learned structure**. Symbolic systems have strong priors and weak data-fit; neural systems have weak priors and strong data-fit.

The non-obvious insight: they're complementary, not rivals — modern *neuro-symbolic* systems (and LLMs using tools/chain-of-thought) reintroduce symbolic scaffolding atop learned perception.
