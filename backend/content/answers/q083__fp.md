question: "What is the difference between a primary key and a foreign key?"

Begin with the deepest problem in data: **identity and reference**. To reason about a world of entities you need two capabilities—to say *"this is a distinct thing"* and to say *"this thing points to that thing."* Primary and foreign keys are exactly these two primitives made formal.

A **primary key** answers identity. It's a column (or set) that is *unique* and *non-null* for every row, guaranteeing each entity has one unambiguous address. Why must it be unique and non-null? Because without uniqueness two rows could be the same entity (you couldn't update one without ambiguity), and a null identity is a thing you can't refer to at all. The PK is the row's name.

A **foreign key** answers reference. It's a column whose values must exist as a primary key in another table—an `orders.customer_id` pointing at `customers.id`. This enforces **referential integrity**: you cannot reference a customer who doesn't exist, and (with constraints) cannot delete a customer while orders point to them.

The unifying principle: keys turn a pile of tables into a **graph of verifiable relationships**. The non-obvious insight—the FK constraint isn't primarily about joining; joins work without it. Its real job is *preventing impossible states*, making "dangling pointers" unrepresentable at the storage layer.
