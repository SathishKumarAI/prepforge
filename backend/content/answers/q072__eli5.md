question: "What is Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)?"
topic: "Data Science"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"

---

Imagine you find footprints in mud and want to guess the shoe size that made them. **Maximum Likelihood Estimation** picks the setting that makes what you actually observed as unsurprising as possible. In other words: of all possible shoe sizes, choose the one under which "these exact prints" would be most expected.

More generally, you assume your data comes from some kind of distribution with unknown knobs (like the average and the spread), then turn those knobs until the observed data looks maximally likely. Because multiplying many probabilities gets messy, people take the logarithm to turn the product into a friendlier sum, then find its peak.

Lots of everyday methods secretly do this — the "best" average of some measurements turns out to be the plain sample mean, and logistic regression is trained this way too. It works beautifully with plenty of data, but can be shaky or biased with only a few points.
