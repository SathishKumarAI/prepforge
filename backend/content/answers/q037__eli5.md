question: "What is k-fold cross-validation and why use it?"
topic: "Machine Learning"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"
sources: []
---

Suppose you have only a small class of students and want a fair read on a teaching method — testing on just one lucky handful could mislead you. **k-fold cross-validation** fixes this by **rotating who takes the test**. Split the data into k equal groups (folds). Train on k-1 of them, test on the one left out, and record the score. Then rotate: a *different* fold sits out and gets tested, while the rest train. Do this k times so **every fold takes its turn as the test**, then average all the scores. The payoff: nobody's judged on a single unlucky split, every data point serves both roles, and you also see how much scores wobble. Handy variants keep class balance in each fold (stratified) or respect time order for time-series data. People usually pick k=5 or 10, trading accuracy against computing cost.
