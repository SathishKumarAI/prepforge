The problem: we have a loss function measuring how wrong a model is, parameterized by possibly millions of weights, and we want the weights that minimize it. We can't check every combination (exponential), and for most models there's no closed-form solution. We need to *search* an enormous space using only local information.

Here's the key fact from calculus: the **gradient** — the vector of partial derivatives — points in the direction of *steepest increase* of the loss. So its negative points in the direction that decreases the loss fastest, locally. Gradient descent exploits exactly this: repeatedly take a small step downhill, θ ← θ − η∇L(θ), where η (the learning rate) sets step size. It's the mathematical formalization of "feel which way is downhill and walk that way."

Why does this scale? Because the gradient is cheap — via backpropagation (the chain rule applied efficiently) — even for huge networks, and it needs only local derivative information, never a global view.

The trade-offs follow directly: too-large η overshoots or diverges; too-small crawls. The gradient only guarantees *local* descent, so on non-convex landscapes we reach local minima or saddle points, not necessarily the global one.

The non-obvious insight: **stochastic** gradient descent — estimating the gradient from small random batches — isn't just a speed hack. The resulting noise helps escape sharp minima and saddle points, and empirically biases learning toward *flatter* minima that generalize better.
