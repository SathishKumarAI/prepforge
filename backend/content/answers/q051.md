The lifecycle runs roughly: business understanding → data acquisition → cleaning/EDA → feature engineering → modeling → evaluation → deployment → monitoring. CRISP-DM is the canonical reference, but the honest version is that it's a loop, not a line — you bounce back constantly.

The stage people skip and regret is the first one: **framing the problem**. Nail down the decision the model informs, the target definition, and the success metric *before* touching data. In production I've seen a churn model that "worked" at 0.85 AUC be worthless because the label ("churn") was defined as 90-day inactivity, but the business could only act on 30-day signals — the prediction arrived too late to matter.

Cleaning and EDA typically eat 60–70% of the calendar. Feature engineering is where domain knowledge pays off more than model choice. Modeling itself is often the fastest part — a baseline (logistic regression, gradient-boosted trees) beats a fancy model more often than juniors expect.

The mistake is treating deployment as the finish line. A model degrades the moment the world drifts from the training distribution, so monitoring (data drift, prediction distribution, business KPI) closes the loop. Budget for retraining from day one; a model with no owner and no monitoring is technical debt, not an asset.
