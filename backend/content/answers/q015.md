My default order is prompting → RAG → fine-tuning, because that's increasing order of cost and commitment. I reach for fine-tuning when the need is about **behavior, not knowledge**: a consistent tone/format the model won't reliably hold from instructions, a specialized skill (say, emitting a strict domain-specific DSL), or classification/extraction where I have thousands of labeled examples and want reliability plus lower latency by shrinking the prompt.

The mistake I see most often is trying to fine-tune facts into a model. Don't. Fine-tuning is a poor, expensive, and stale way to inject knowledge — the model will still hallucinate around the edges, and every fact update means retraining. For "what does our policy say today," that's RAG's job.

A useful gut check: if better examples in the prompt fix it, you didn't need fine-tuning. If the model *knows what to do but won't consistently do it*, fine-tuning helps. If it *lacks the information*, RAG helps.

In practice I almost always use **parameter-efficient fine-tuning** — LoRA/QLoRA — training small adapter weights instead of the full model. It cuts GPU cost dramatically, adapters are swappable, and it avoids catastrophic forgetting of general ability. The realistic architecture for a serious product is often *both*: light fine-tuning to lock in style and task behavior, RAG to supply current facts. They solve different problems and stack cleanly.
