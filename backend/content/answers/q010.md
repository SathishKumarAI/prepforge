NLP is the branch of AI concerned with getting machines to understand, interpret, and generate human language — text and speech. It sits at the messy intersection of linguistics and statistics because language is ambiguous, context-dependent, and full of long-range dependencies.

The common tasks span a rough easy-to-hard gradient:

- **Tokenization** and normalization — splitting text into words/subwords (BPE, WordPiece today).
- **POS tagging** and **parsing** — grammatical structure.
- **Named Entity Recognition (NER)** — pulling out people, orgs, dates.
- **Text classification / sentiment analysis** — topic or opinion labels.
- **Machine translation**, **summarization**, and **question answering** — sequence-to-sequence generation.
- **Semantic search / embeddings** — retrieval by meaning, the backbone of RAG.

The field went through a hard architectural break. Classical pipelines leaned on bag-of-words, TF-IDF, and hand-engineered features feeding SVMs or CRFs; then RNNs/LSTMs; and since 2017 **transformers** (BERT for understanding, GPT-family for generation) dominate by learning contextual representations from massive corpora. That shift collapsed a lot of task-specific engineering into "fine-tune or prompt a pretrained model."

In production the unglamorous parts still bite: tokenization edge cases, encoding/Unicode issues, and evaluation. Sentiment "accuracy" hides sarcasm and domain shift; a model trained on product reviews falls over on clinical notes. I always budget for domain-specific eval sets rather than trusting benchmark numbers.
