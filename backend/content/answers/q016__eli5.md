question: "What is hallucination in LLMs and how can it be mitigated?"
topic: "AI"

Picture a student who never says 'I don't know.' Asked anything, they answer smoothly and confidently, even when they're completely making it up. That's a **hallucination** in an AI language model: text that sounds fluent and sure but is actually wrong or invented, like fake book citations or made-up dates. It happens because the model was trained to produce *plausible-sounding* next words, not to check whether they're true; it has no built-in fact-checker. You can reduce it, though. Let the model look up real sources before answering (retrieval). Tell it to cite evidence or admit when it's unsure. Turn down its 'creativity' setting so it plays it safe. Add checks that verify claims, or give it tools like a calculator. And train it on high-quality, honest examples. None of this fully cures the problem, so for anything important, keep a human reviewing the output.
