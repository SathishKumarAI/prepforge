question: "What is the multiple comparisons problem and how do you correct for it?"

Run one statistical test and there's a small chance — say 5% — you'll see a "significant" result purely by luck. Now run *twenty* tests. It's like buying twenty lottery tickets: each has slim odds, but across all of them the chance that at least one is a fluke shoots up to about 64%. That's the **multiple comparisons problem** — test enough things and something will look exciting just by chance.

The fix is to raise the bar. **Bonferroni** simply demands a much stricter cutoff — divide your 5% by the number of tests — so flukes rarely sneak through, though it's so strict you may miss real effects. **Holm** is a gentler stepwise version of the same idea. **Benjamini-Hochberg** instead controls the *fraction* of your "discoveries" that are false, which is more forgiving and popular when you're screening thousands of things.

Choose based on how much a single false alarm would cost you.
