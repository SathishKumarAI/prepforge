An effective dashboard **answers a specific set of questions for a specific audience at a glance, and drives a decision**. It's a product, not a data dump — the failure mode is the "everything dashboard" with forty charts that nobody trusts and everyone ignores.

What separates good from bad:

- **Purpose and audience first.** An exec dashboard shows three or four KPIs against targets with trend context. An operational dashboard for an on-call team is denser and real-time. Don't blend the two.
- **Inverted-pyramid layout.** Most important numbers top-left (where eyes land first), supporting detail below, drill-downs on demand. Follow the natural reading order.
- **Context on every metric.** A bare number is useless — show the comparison: vs. last period, vs. target, and the trend. "$1.2M" means nothing; "$1.2M, +8% WoW, 4% under target" means something.
- **Trust.** Clear definitions, a visible "last refreshed" timestamp, and consistent numbers with other reports. The fastest way to kill a dashboard is one wrong number — people stop believing all of it.

Keep it visually calm: minimal colors, color used to signal (red/green for status) not decorate, and no chartjunk. In production I've killed more dashboards than I've built — most add value by being ruthlessly pruned. If a chart hasn't influenced a decision in a quarter, remove it. Fast load times matter too; a dashboard that takes 30 seconds to render won't get looked at.
