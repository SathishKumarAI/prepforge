k-means partitions data into `k` clusters by minimizing within-cluster squared distance. It alternates two steps until convergence: **assign** each point to its nearest centroid, then **update** each centroid to the mean of its assigned points. Repeat until assignments stop changing. That's Lloyd's algorithm, and it always converges — but only to a **local** optimum.

Because it's local, initialization matters a lot. Plain random seeding can land on bad partitions, so use **k-means++** (the scikit-learn default), which spreads initial centroids apart, and run multiple restarts (`n_init`) keeping the best inertia.

The practical gotchas:

- **You must pick k up front.** The elbow method (inertia vs. k) and silhouette score guide it, but there's usually judgment involved.
- **Scale your features** — it's a distance algorithm, so an unscaled large-range feature dominates the clustering.
- **It assumes spherical, similar-sized clusters.** Elongated, nested, or density-varying shapes break it; that's when I reach for DBSCAN or a Gaussian Mixture Model.
- **Outliers drag centroids** because the mean isn't robust.

In production I've seen k-means "work" and produce meaningless segments simply because nobody scaled the inputs and one column was in the millions. Always standardize, always run it several times, and sanity-check that the clusters map to something a stakeholder recognizes — a clustering that doesn't tell a story usually isn't real.
