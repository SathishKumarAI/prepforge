question: "How does backpropagation work?"

Imagine a factory assembly line where the final product comes out flawed. To improve, you trace the blame **backward**: how much did the last station contribute to the error, then the one before it, and so on, until every station knows its share. **Backpropagation** does exactly this for a neural network.

First comes a **forward pass**: data flows through the layers to produce a prediction, and you measure the error (the loss). Then the **backward pass** uses the chain rule from calculus to push that error backward, computing how much each weight contributed. Every weight then nudges itself in the direction that shrinks the error.

The clever part is that it reuses calculations from later layers when handling earlier ones, which makes it fast. Backprop only *finds* the blame; a separate optimizer (like SGD or Adam) actually applies the adjustments, step after step, until the network learns.
