The fundamental problem: a chart is a **perceptual encoding**. You map data values onto visual channels—position, length, angle, area, color—and the reader *decodes* them back. Charting well means choosing encodings the human visual system decodes with least error.

That reframes the question from taste to psychophysics. Cleveland and McGill ranked how accurately people judge visual channels: **position along a common scale** is most accurate, then length, then angle/slope, then area, then color/volume. This *derives* most chart advice. A bar chart beats a pie chart not by convention but because comparing bar lengths on a shared axis is a more accurate judgment than comparing pie slice angles. Never encode a quantity as area when position is available.

Next, match the encoding to the *question*, which follows from data type:
- **Comparison across categories** → bars (position/length).
- **Trend over time** → line (slope reveals rate of change).
- **Relationship between two variables** → scatter (2D position).
- **Distribution** → histogram/boxplot.
- **Part-to-whole** → stacked bar, rarely pie.

The non-obvious insight: the chart's job is to *offload cognition onto perception*. If the reader must compute a comparison in their head, you chose the wrong encoding—the answer should be *seen*, not calculated.
