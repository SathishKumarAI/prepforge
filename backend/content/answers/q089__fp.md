Start from a simple truth about any multi-step goal: **conversion is a product of survival probabilities**. If reaching purchase requires visit → cart → checkout → pay, the overall rate is `p₁ × p₂ × p₃`. Because these multiply, a chain is only as strong as its weakest link—and small leaks compound catastrophically. Three steps at 80% each yield just 51% overall.

Funnel analysis is the discipline of *decomposing that product back into its factors*. You measure the fraction surviving each ordered step, exposing exactly *where* users drop and *how much* each stage costs. This converts a vague "conversion is low" into a located, quantified leak.

Why is locating the leak so valuable? Because of the multiplicative structure, effort should target the **step with the steepest drop and the most traffic still present**—fixing a 90%→40% collapse early beats optimizing a late step that few reach. Funnels turn optimization into a search for maximum marginal return.

How to use it: define ordered steps, count unique users reaching each, chart the decay, then segment (by device, source, cohort) to ask *who* leaks and *why*—a mobile-only checkout drop points straight at a broken form.

The non-obvious insight: funnel steps are rarely truly sequential or instantaneous. Users loop, skip, and return days later, so your **attribution window and step-ordering assumptions** silently shape the numbers—two analysts can measure the "same" funnel and disagree entirely.
