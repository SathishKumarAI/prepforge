question: "How does the Naive Bayes classifier work and what is the 'naive' assumption?"

We want `P(class | features)` but can only easily estimate the reverse. Bayes' theorem flips it: `P(y | x) ∝ P(x | y) · P(y)`. To classify, we just pick the class maximizing this product, so we never need the normalizing denominator.

The obstacle is `P(x | y)` — the joint distribution over all features given the class. With `d` features each taking `k` values, that's `kᵈ` probabilities to estimate: the curse of dimensionality makes it impossible from finite data.

The "naive" assumption slices the knot: **assume features are conditionally independent given the class.** Then the joint factorizes into a product of one-dimensional terms, `P(x|y) = Πⱼ P(xⱼ|y)`. Now you estimate only `d·k` numbers — each feature's per-class histogram — which is trivial. That single assumption converts an intractable joint into `d` easy marginals.

Why does a *clearly false* assumption (features are usually correlated) work so well? Because classification only needs the *argmax* to be right, not the probabilities to be accurate. Correlations distort the magnitude of the posterior but often leave the *ranking* of classes intact.

Non-obvious insight: Naive Bayes is a **linear classifier** in log-space — taking logs turns the product into a sum of per-feature evidence, so it's spam-filter-fast, needs little data, yet its confidence scores are notoriously overconfident and should not be trusted as calibrated probabilities.
