The **population** is the entire set of units you actually care about — every customer, every transaction, every possible user. A **sample** is a subset you observe. Statistics you compute on a population are **parameters** (μ, σ); the same quantities on a sample are **estimates** (x̄, s), and inference is the machinery for reasoning from estimate back to parameter with a stated uncertainty.

We sample because measuring the whole population is usually impossible, too slow, or too expensive — you can't survey every future customer, and you can't run an experiment on all of them. Even when you *can* process everything (big data), you often sample anyway to prototype fast or fit a model that won't scale to billions of rows.

The part that matters more than sample *size* is sample *representativeness*. A biased sample of a million is worse than an unbiased sample of a thousand. The 1936 *Literary Digest* poll had 2.4 million responses and still called the election wrong because its frame (phone and car owners) skewed wealthy. In production I've seen "we trained on all our data" mask a brutal selection bias — the training set was only users who converted, so the model never saw the population it had to score. Random sampling, stratification for rare subgroups, and honest accounting for how the data was collected matter more than raw volume.
