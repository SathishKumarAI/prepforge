An MDP is the mathematical framework for sequential decision-making under uncertainty — the formal foundation nearly all of reinforcement learning is built on. It's the tuple `(S, A, P, R, γ)`: states `S`, actions `A`, a transition model `P(s'|s,a)` giving the probability of landing in `s'` after taking `a` in `s`, a reward function `R(s,a)`, and a discount factor `γ ∈ [0,1]` that weights how much future reward matters versus immediate reward.

The defining assumption is the **Markov property**: the next state and reward depend only on the *current* state and action, not the full history. That's what makes the problem tractable — you don't need to remember the whole trajectory, just enough state to be predictive.

The objective is a **policy** `π(a|s)` that maximizes expected discounted return. Everything ties together through the Bellman equation:

`V(s) = max_a [ R(s,a) + γ · Σ P(s'|s,a) V(s') ]`

When you know `P` and `R`, you solve it with dynamic programming — value iteration or policy iteration. When you don't (the usual real-world case), you drop to model-free RL like Q-learning or policy gradients that learn from sampled experience.

The practical art is state design. Two things bite in production: choosing a state representation that's actually Markov (or accepting a POMDP and adding memory), and setting `γ` — too low and the agent is myopic, too close to 1 and value estimates become high-variance and slow to converge.
