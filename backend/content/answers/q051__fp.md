Don't memorize a stage list — *derive* it from the single constraint that a data science project must convert a real-world decision into a defensible answer, and every stage exists to remove one specific way that conversion can fail.

- **Problem framing.** The first failure is solving the wrong problem. So you translate a business question into a measurable target and a success metric. Skip this and everything downstream is precise but pointless.
- **Data acquisition & understanding.** A model can only know what the data encodes. You gather sources and interrogate their provenance, because unknown biases here become invisible errors later.
- **Cleaning & preparation.** Real data violates the i.i.d., complete, correctly-typed assumptions models rely on. This stage — usually the largest — forces reality to match those assumptions.
- **EDA.** Before modeling, you check what structure actually exists, so you don't fit a model to a pattern that isn't there.
- **Modeling.** Only now: fit, compare, tune.
- **Evaluation.** Test against the *business* metric on held-out data, guarding against overfitting and leakage.
- **Deployment & monitoring.** Value is realized only in production, and distributions drift, so monitoring closes the loop.

Non-obvious insight: the process is **iterative, not linear** — evaluation routinely sends you back to reframe. And the earliest, least-technical stage (framing) determines success far more than the choice of algorithm.
