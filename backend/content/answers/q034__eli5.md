question: "How does the k-means clustering algorithm work?"
topic: "Machine Learning"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"
sources: []
---

**k-means** sorts a pile of unlabeled data into groups, like organizing a crowd at a party into conversation circles. You decide up front how many circles you want — that's **k**. Then it runs a simple loop. First, drop k "hosts" (centroids) randomly into the room. Second, **every guest walks to the nearest host**, forming rough circles. Third, **each host repositions to the exact middle of their circle**. Guests then re-check who's now closest and may switch circles, hosts recenter again, and this repeats until nobody moves — the groups have settled. A few catches: you must guess k in advance (tools like the "elbow method" help), results depend on where the hosts started (so people try several restarts), and you should scale features first. It also assumes tidy, roughly round, similar-sized clumps, so oddly shaped groups can trip it up.
