Skewness measures the **asymmetry** of a distribution — formally the standardized third moment, E[(X−μ)³]/σ³. The third power is the key: cubing preserves sign, so values far out on one tail dominate and the statistic reports which side has the long tail. Symmetric data has skew 0; a right (positive) skew means a heavy upper tail, and there mean > median because the mean is dragged by extremes while the median isn't.

Why does it matter mechanically? Many methods carry a hidden Gaussian assumption. Linear/logistic regression assume roughly symmetric errors; distance-based methods (k-NN, k-means) and gradient descent let a few huge values dominate the loss and distort the geometry. Skew concentrates most points in a small range and lets rare extremes pull the fit. It's fundamentally a *scale* mismatch: the feature lives on a multiplicative scale but the model reasons additively.

The fix follows from that diagnosis — change the scale. Monotonic concave transforms (log, √, Box-Cox, Yeo-Johnson) compress large values more than small ones, converting multiplicative spread into additive spread and pulling the tail in. Log is natural when a variable spans orders of magnitude (income, counts).

The insight most miss: skew is often a *feature*, not a defect. Power-law/log-normal data (wealth, city sizes) is *supposed* to be skewed; transform because the *model* needs it, not reflexively — and tree-based models, being scale-invariant, don't care at all.
