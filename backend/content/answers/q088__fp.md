The problem cohort analysis solves is a **confound of time**. An aggregate metric like "monthly active users" blends two populations that behave nothing alike: brand-new users still in their honeymoon, and veterans who long ago churned or matured. Averaging over them smears distinct life-cycles into one meaningless line. Worse, a flood of new signups can make retention *look* healthy even as every individual user is leaving faster.

Cohort analysis removes the confound by **holding start-time constant**. You group users by a shared origin event—usually signup month—and track each group's behavior over its *own* elapsed time (week 1, week 2, …). This is exactly the epidemiologist's trick: to study a treatment's effect, follow a fixed group forward rather than snapshotting a churning crowd.

Why is this the right decomposition? Because it separates **age effects** (how behavior changes as a user matures) from **cohort effects** (whether users acquired in March are intrinsically better than April's) from **calendar effects** (a site-wide outage hitting everyone at once). Aggregate metrics tangle all three; the cohort grid untangles them.

The non-obvious insight: cohorts reveal whether product changes actually *worked*. If each successive cohort's week-4 retention climbs, your improvements are compounding—a signal completely invisible in a single blended metric that a growing top-of-funnel can mask indefinitely.
