Computer vision is the field of AI that gets machines to extract meaning from images and video — turning a grid of pixels into structured understanding (what's in the scene, where it is, and what it's doing).

The core tasks form a natural hierarchy of granularity:

- **Image classification** — one label for the whole image ("cat").
- **Object detection** — classify *and* localize with bounding boxes (YOLO, Faster R-CNN).
- **Semantic segmentation** — label every pixel by class; **instance segmentation** (Mask R-CNN) separates individual objects.
- **Pose estimation, OCR, face/landmark recognition, and image generation/inpainting** round out the common ones.

Architecturally, CNNs dominated for a decade by exploiting spatial locality and translation invariance, and Vision Transformers (ViT) now compete or win at scale, with CLIP-style vision-language models enabling zero-shot recognition. Applications span autonomous driving, medical imaging, industrial inspection, retail, and AR.

Where projects actually die is rarely the model and almost always the data and the domain gap. In production I've seen a detector benchmark at 95% mAP and then fall apart on-site because the training images were clean and daylit while the deployment camera sees glare, motion blur, and odd angles. The unglamorous work — labeling quality, class imbalance, hard-negative mining, and evaluating on data that matches deployment conditions — determines success far more than picking YOLOv8 over v9. Budget for a real annotation and edge-case pipeline before you obsess over architecture.
