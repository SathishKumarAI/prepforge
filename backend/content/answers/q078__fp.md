Raw data lives in many source systems in incompatible shapes; analytics needs it unified, clean, and queryable in one place. Both ETL and ELT do three operations — **Extract** (pull from sources), **Transform** (clean, join, reshape, conform), **Load** (write to a warehouse). The *only* difference is the order of Transform and Load — and that ordering is a direct consequence of where compute is cheap.

- **ETL (Extract → Transform → Load):** transform *before* loading, on a separate processing tier. This is the historical default because warehouse storage and compute were scarce and expensive — you filtered and pre-shaped data so you only stored the finished, modeled result. You pay for cleanliness up front; the warehouse holds curated data only.
- **ELT (Extract → Load → Transform):** dump raw data into the warehouse first, then transform *in place* using its engine (SQL). This became viable only when cloud warehouses (Snowflake, BigQuery) made storage cheap and compute elastic and massively parallel.

The principle: architecture follows the economics of compute. When compute moved into the warehouse and got cheap, it made sense to move the transformation there too.

The insight most miss: ELT isn't just "reordered ETL" — it changes *what's possible*. Keeping raw data means transformations are re-runnable and auditable, you can reshape for new questions without re-extracting, and schema-on-read lets you defer modeling decisions. The cost is governance: raw lakes can rot into swamps without discipline.
