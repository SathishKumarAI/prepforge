question: "What does GROUP BY do in SQL, and how does HAVING differ from WHERE?"
topic: "Data Analytics"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"
sources: []

**GROUP BY** takes a big table and sorts its rows into buckets that share a value — say, one bucket per department — so you can compute a summary for each bucket, like a headcount or an average salary. Instead of many rows, you get one tidy row per group.

The tricky part is filtering, and there are two moments to do it. **WHERE** filters the *individual people before* they're sorted into buckets — "ignore anyone who left the company." **HAVING** filters the *finished buckets after* the summarizing — "only show departments whose average salary tops $50,000." Since that average doesn't even exist until the buckets are formed, WHERE can't use it, but HAVING can.

So the flow is: WHERE weeds out rows first, GROUP BY forms the buckets, then HAVING weeds out whole buckets. Getting this order right is the key to correct summaries — filter raw rows with WHERE, filter grouped results with HAVING.
