These are the knobs that control how random the next-token pick is — they don't touch the model, only how you draw from its probability distribution.

**Temperature** scales the logits before softmax (`logits / T`). `T < 1` sharpens the distribution toward the top candidates (more deterministic, more conservative); `T > 1` flattens it (more diverse, more risk of nonsense); `T → 0` is greedy decoding, always the argmax. **Top-k** truncates to the k most probable tokens, renormalizes, then samples — it kills the long tail but with a *fixed* cutoff, which is clumsy: when the model is very confident, k=50 still admits junk; when it's uncertain, k=50 may be too few. **Top-p (nucleus)** fixes that by taking the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability ≥ p, then sampling. The candidate set *adapts* — narrow when the model is confident, wide when it's not — which is why it's usually preferred over top-k.

The practical mapping I use: for anything factual or structured — extraction, classification, code, JSON output — I run **temperature 0 or near-0** so results are reproducible and grounded. For creative or brainstorming tasks I raise temperature (~0.7–1.0) and set top-p around 0.9 to keep it coherent while allowing variety. Temperature and top-p compose. And a reliability point that trips people up: at T=0 you get *deterministic* decoding, but sampling-based outputs aren't reproducible unless you also fix the seed.
