Bayes' theorem is the unique consistent rule for **updating belief when evidence arrives** — it's not a formula to memorize but the only arithmetic that keeps probabilities coherent. It answers: given that I saw evidence E, how likely is hypothesis H?

It falls out of a symmetry. The joint probability P(H and E) can be decomposed two ways: P(H)·P(E|H) = P(E)·P(H|E). Solve for the term you want:

**P(H|E) = P(E|H)·P(H) / P(E)**

The deep point is that it *reverses conditioning*. We usually know P(E|H) — the probability of a symptom given a disease — but we *want* P(H|E), the disease given the symptom. Those two are wildly different, and confusing them is the base-rate fallacy.

Example: a disease affects 1 in 1,000. A test is 99% accurate (1% false positives). You test positive — are you sick? Intuition screams 99%. But of 1,000 people, ~1 is truly sick (tests positive), while ~10 healthy people also test positive (1% of 999). So P(sick | positive) ≈ 1/11 ≈ **9%**. The rare prior overwhelms the accurate test.

The insight most miss: evidence doesn't set your belief, it *multiplies* your prior. A very strong likelihood against a very small base rate still yields a small posterior — which is exactly why context and priors are not optional.
