Feature selection falls into three families — filter, wrapper, and embedded — plus dimensionality reduction as a related but distinct option.

**Filter methods** score each feature against the target independently of any model: correlation, mutual information, chi-square, ANOVA F-test, variance thresholds. They're fast and model-agnostic, good for a first pass on wide data, but they miss interactions and redundancy (two correlated features both score high even though one is enough).

**Wrapper methods** search subsets by actually training a model and scoring it — recursive feature elimination (RFE), forward/backward stepwise selection. They capture interactions and optimize for your real model, but they're expensive and can overfit the selection to your validation set if you're not careful with nested CV.

**Embedded methods** do selection during training. **L1/Lasso** drives coefficients to exactly zero; tree ensembles give feature importances (though impurity-based importance is biased toward high-cardinality features — prefer permutation importance or SHAP). This is my default in production: it's nearly free and jointly optimized with the model.

Separately, **PCA** and autoencoders reduce dimensionality by *combining* features rather than selecting them — great for compression, terrible for interpretability.

The honest advice: don't over-engineer this. Modern gradient-boosted trees tolerate irrelevant features well, so I usually reach for L1 or permutation importance and reserve aggressive selection for cases with genuine cost, latency, or interpretability constraints.
