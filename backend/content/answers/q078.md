**ETL — Extract, Transform, Load** — is the traditional pattern: pull data from sources, transform it (clean, join, aggregate, conform to a schema) on a separate processing tier, then load the finished result into the warehouse. **ELT — Extract, Load, Transform** — swaps the last two steps: land the **raw** data in the warehouse first, then transform it *in place* using the warehouse's own compute.

The reordering isn't cosmetic — it reflects where compute got cheap. ETL was born when warehouses were expensive and rigid, so you did heavy lifting on dedicated ETL servers and only loaded clean, modeled data. ELT is the modern default because cloud warehouses (**Snowflake, BigQuery, Redshift**) have massive, elastic, cheap compute — so it's easier to dump everything in and transform with SQL, typically orchestrated by **dbt**.

Why I usually prefer ELT today: you **keep the raw data**, so when requirements change (and they always do) you re-transform from source without re-ingesting. Transformations live as version-controlled SQL, which is testable and reviewable. And you get schema-on-read flexibility for semi-structured data.

When ETL still wins: when you *must* cleanse or mask before the data lands — PII redaction for compliance, or you legally can't store raw sensitive fields — and when the destination has limited compute. In practice most teams I've worked with run ELT for analytics and keep targeted ETL for regulated or sensitive fields. Both aim at the same end: analysis-ready data. The question is just *where* the transform compute runs and whether you retain the raw copy.
