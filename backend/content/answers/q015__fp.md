The decision follows from *where knowledge lives* and *what kind of change you need*. Three levers exist: prompting (change the input), RAG (change the retrievable context), and fine-tuning (change the weights). They fix different things, so the question is really *what is failing?*

Prompting and RAG both inject information at inference time — they're ideal when the gap is **knowledge**: the model lacks facts, or needs current/private data. RAG especially suits knowledge that changes often, because updating a document store is instant while retraining is not.

Fine-tuning is warranted when the gap is **behavior, not facts** — when you need to reshape the model's *default distribution* itself: consistent tone or format, a specialized skill or domain style, structured outputs the base model resists, or compressing a long, expensive prompt into learned weights for latency and cost. It's also the answer when few-shot examples would overflow the context window; fine-tuning amortizes them into parameters.

Why the distinction is principled: prompts and retrieval steer a fixed function; fine-tuning *moves the function*. You can't prompt your way to a genuinely new behavioral prior, and you shouldn't fine-tune to memorize volatile facts.

The non-obvious insight: these compose. The strongest systems fine-tune for *form and skill*, then use RAG for *substance* — teaching the model how to behave while feeding it what to know.
