Gradient descent is the workhorse optimizer that trains almost everything from linear regression to LLMs. You want to minimize a loss `J(θ)`; the gradient `∇J(θ)` points in the direction of steepest *increase*, so you step the opposite way:

`θ ← θ − α · ∇J(θ)`

where `α` is the learning rate. Repeat until convergence. That's it conceptually — the engineering is in the variants and the learning rate.

**Variants:** batch GD uses the whole dataset per step (stable, slow, and often won't fit in memory); **stochastic** GD uses one sample (fast, very noisy); **mini-batch** uses a small batch (say 32–256) and is what everyone actually uses — it's the sweet spot for GPU throughput and gradient stability.

The **learning rate** is the hyperparameter that makes or breaks training. Too high and the loss oscillates or diverges (NaNs); too low and it crawls or stalls in a bad region. In practice nobody hand-tunes a single value — you use an **adaptive optimizer** like **Adam**, which keeps per-parameter momentum and scales steps by recent gradient magnitude, plus a **learning-rate schedule** (warmup then decay). That combination is why modern deep nets train at all.

One clarification I always add: for convex losses GD finds the global minimum, but neural-net losses are wildly non-convex — GD only finds a *good local* minimum. That it works so well anyway is one of deep learning's happy empirical surprises, not a guarantee.
