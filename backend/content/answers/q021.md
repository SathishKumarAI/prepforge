question: "Why are Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) well-suited for images?"
topic: "AI"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"
sources: []

CNNs bake in the right *inductive biases* for images, which is why they beat fully connected nets on pixels with far less data. Three ideas do the work.

**Local receptive fields:** a convolutional filter looks at a small patch, so early layers detect local structure — edges, corners, textures — and deeper layers compose those into parts and objects. This matches how images are actually built: meaning is local before it's global. **Parameter sharing:** the *same* filter slides across the whole image. A fully connected layer on a 224×224 image needs tens of thousands of weights per neuron; a conv filter needs a handful, reused everywhere. That's a massive reduction in parameters and gives **translation equivariance** — a cat is detected whether it's top-left or center. **Pooling:** downsampling adds tolerance to small shifts and enlarges the effective receptive field cheaply.

Stack conv → nonlinearity → pooling and you get a learned feature hierarchy, edges to objects, with the network discovering the features instead of you hand-crafting them.

The nuance I'd add in an interview: these biases are a gift when data is limited but a *ceiling* when it isn't. **Vision Transformers** have fewer built-in assumptions and now match or beat CNNs — but they typically need much more data or heavy augmentation and pretraining to get there. So the honest answer is CNNs are well-suited to images *and* data-efficient because their priors align with image structure; that same rigidity is why ViTs overtake them at scale.
