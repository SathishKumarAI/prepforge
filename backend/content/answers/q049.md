question: "How does the Naive Bayes classifier work and what is the 'naive' assumption?"

Naive Bayes is a probabilistic classifier built on Bayes' theorem. It computes the posterior probability of each class given the features, `P(class | features) ∝ P(class) · P(features | class)`, and predicts the class with the highest posterior. The **prior** `P(class)` comes from class frequencies; the **likelihood** `P(features | class)` comes from the training data.

The "naive" part is the assumption that **all features are conditionally independent given the class**. That lets it factor the joint likelihood into a simple product of per-feature likelihoods, `∏ P(xᵢ | class)`, instead of modeling their interactions — which is what makes it so cheap to train and score. The assumption is almost always false in reality (words in a document clearly aren't independent), yet the classifier works surprisingly well because it only needs the *ranking* of posteriors to be right, not their exact values.

Variants match the data: **Multinomial NB** for word counts, **Bernoulli NB** for binary features, **Gaussian NB** for continuous features. One must-have is **Laplace (additive) smoothing** — without it, a single feature value never seen with a class produces a zero likelihood that annihilates the whole product.

In production I still reach for it as a fast, strong **text-classification baseline** (spam filtering, topic tagging) — it trains in one pass, scales to huge vocabularies, and needs almost no tuning. Its main weakness beyond the independence assumption is that its probability estimates are poorly calibrated, so trust the predicted class, not the confidence number.
