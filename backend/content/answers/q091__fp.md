question: "What is database normalization and what problem does it solve?"
topic: "Data Analytics"

The root problem is **redundancy breeds contradiction**. If a fact—"customer 42 lives in Ohio"—is stored in many rows, then updating it means updating all of them atomically. Miss one and the database now asserts two contradictory truths. These are the classic **update, insertion, and deletion anomalies**: change data inconsistently, be unable to record a fact without an unrelated one, or lose a fact by deleting a row.

Normalization is the systematic cure: **store every fact exactly once**, in the one place it logically belongs. The formal machinery—functional dependencies and normal forms—is just precision about "where a fact belongs."

- **1NF**: atomic values, no repeating groups.
- **2NF**: non-key attributes depend on the *whole* key, not part of it.
- **3NF**: non-key attributes depend on the key and *nothing but the key* (remove transitive dependencies).

The unifying principle is a **single source of truth**: each functional dependency `A → B` gets its own table so B is written once. This is really information theory—eliminate redundant encoding, and you eliminate the possibility of the copies disagreeing.

The non-obvious insight: normalization optimizes for *write correctness*, and it directly costs *read performance* (more joins). That's precisely why analytics deliberately **de**normalizes—when data is append-only and read-heavy, the anomaly risk vanishes and join-avoidance wins. Normal form is a tool, not a virtue.
