EDA is the disciplined first look at a dataset — understanding its shape, quality, and relationships before you commit to any modeling assumptions. Tukey's original framing still holds: you're letting the data surprise you rather than confirming what you expected.

Concretely it involves: distributions of each variable (histograms, KDEs, value counts), summary stats (mean/median/quantiles, missingness rates), relationships between variables (scatterplots, correlation matrices, group-by aggregations), and the target's relationship to features. `pandas` `.describe()` and `.info()`, `df.isna().sum()`, and a few `seaborn` pairplots get you 80% of the way. Tools like `ydata-profiling` (formerly pandas-profiling) automate a first pass.

What you're hunting for: missing values and their pattern (missing-at-random vs. structural), outliers, skew that will need transforming, high-cardinality categoricals, leakage (a feature that's suspiciously predictive), class imbalance, and duplicate or inconsistent records.

The mistake juniors make is jumping to modeling and treating EDA as a formality. In production I've caught leakage in EDA that would've shipped a fraudulent 0.99-AUC model — a `payment_status` column was populated *after* the event we were predicting. Ten minutes of plotting target-vs-feature correlations exposed it. EDA is also where you sanity-check that the data actually matches the business's mental model; mismatches there are cheaper to find now than after deployment.
