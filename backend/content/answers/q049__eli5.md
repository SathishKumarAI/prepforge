question: "How does the Naive Bayes classifier work and what is the 'naive' assumption?"

Imagine sorting mail into "spam" or "not spam" by looking at each word separately. You know "lottery" shows up mostly in spam and "meeting" mostly in real mail, so you tally each word's vote and pick the class that seems most likely overall. That's **Naive Bayes**: it uses **Bayes' theorem** to blend a starting guess (how common spam is) with the evidence from the features to compute which class is most probable.

The **"naive"** part is a big simplifying assumption: it pretends every feature is **independent** of the others given the class — as if the words "free" and "money" had nothing to do with each other. That's rarely true, yet the shortcut works remarkably well, especially for text.

It's fast and needs little data. One fix, **Laplace smoothing**, adds a tiny count to every word so a never-before-seen word doesn't crash the whole prediction to zero.
