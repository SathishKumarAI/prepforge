question: "In an analytics context, how do you know if a change in a metric is statistically significant or just noise?"
topic: "Data Analytics"

A metric wiggling up or down between two weeks might be a real change, or just random luck, like flipping a coin ten times and getting six heads. Six isn't proof the coin is rigged; small samples bounce around on their own.

To tell signal from noise: first, know the metric's normal day-to-day wobble before reacting to any single bump. When you can, run a proper **A/B test**, splitting people into two groups, so seasonality or a marketing push can't secretly explain the difference.

Then apply a **hypothesis test** suited to the metric (a z-test for conversion rates, a t-test for averages) and check the **p-value** against a threshold you set in advance, like 0.05. Report a **confidence interval** too, since it shows both whether the effect is real and how big it is.

Make sure your sample is large enough, and beware peeking early or confusing "statistically real" with "actually meaningful."
