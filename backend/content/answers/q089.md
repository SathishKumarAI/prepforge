**Funnel analysis** tracks how users progress through a defined sequence of steps toward a goal — say `visit → sign up → add payment → first purchase` — and measures the **conversion rate between each step** so you can find where people drop off. The value is diagnostic: it turns "conversion is low" into "we lose 60% of users specifically at the add-payment step," which is something a team can act on.

How I use it in practice:

1. **Define the steps and the window.** Steps must be ordered, mutually exclusive events, and you need a conversion window (does step 3 count if it happens 30 days later?). Ambiguity here produces numbers two analysts will disagree on.
2. **Compute step-to-step and overall conversion**, then look at the *biggest absolute drop*, not just the smallest rate — a 10% loss on a step that 90% of users reach costs more than a 40% loss on a step only 5% reach.
3. **Segment the funnel.** The aggregate hides everything. Split by device, channel, or new-vs-returning; often the mobile checkout is quietly hemorrhaging users while desktop looks fine.

Two decisions matter a lot: **strict vs. any-order** funnels (must steps happen in exact sequence?) and **first-touch vs. any-touch** attribution of a step to a user. Tools like Amplitude, Mixpanel, or GA4 do this out of the box; in SQL it's self-joins or window functions over an events table. The common failure I've seen is treating a drop-off as a bug when it's actually intended filtering — not every "leak" is a leak.
