Reduce it to the simplest possible objective: given a sequence of tokens, predict the next one. That's the entire training task — and everything an LLM appears to "know" is a side effect of getting good at it. Why does one trivial objective suffice? Because to predict the next token in *arbitrary* text, a model must implicitly learn grammar, facts, arithmetic, and reasoning — they're all statistically useful for prediction. Compression of language forces understanding.

Mechanically: text is split into **tokens**, mapped to vectors, and passed through stacked **transformer** layers whose self-attention lets each position aggregate context from all others. The final layer outputs a probability distribution over the vocabulary — a score for every possible next token. Generation is then **autoregressive**: sample one token from that distribution, append it, feed the extended sequence back in, and repeat. The model consumes its own output, so coherence emerges token by token.

Sampling strategy (temperature, top-p) controls the trade-off between determinism and diversity by reshaping that distribution before drawing.

The non-obvious insight: the model has no plan and no memory beyond its context window — it is a *stateless* function recomputed at every step. Apparent long-range intention is an illusion produced by conditioning each new prediction on the entire growing prefix. Coherence is reconstructed, not stored.
