question: "What is the difference between a population and a sample, and why do we sample?"
topic: "Data Science"

To know if a big pot of soup is seasoned right, you don't drink the entire pot — you stir it and taste **one spoonful**. The whole pot is the **population** (everything you actually care about); the spoonful is the **sample** (the small part you inspect). A well-stirred spoonful tells you about the whole pot.

Why sample at all? Examining the full population is often too **expensive, slow, or impossible** — you can't survey every customer on Earth. A good sample lets you *infer* the whole thing efficiently.

But the spoonful only works if the soup is stirred. **Random sampling** gives everyone an equal chance; **stratified sampling** makes sure each subgroup (say, age brackets) is fairly represented. Bad, biased sampling — like tasting only the broth on top — misleads you.

Numbers from a sample, like its average, are just **estimates** of the true population values, always carrying some uncertainty.
