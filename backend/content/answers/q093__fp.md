question: "Why can averages be misleading, and what should you report alongside them?"

The mean is a **lossy compression** of a distribution down to a single number—and the direction of information loss is what makes it dangerous. The arithmetic mean minimizes squared error, so it is dragged toward extreme values; a handful of outliers can move it arbitrarily far from where most of the data actually lives. In a right-skewed distribution (incomes, response times, purchase sizes—almost everything in business), the mean sits well above the typical case, so "average" describes *no one*.

Worse, the mean presumes a mental model of a single, roughly symmetric hump. When the true distribution is **multimodal**—two customer segments, say—the average lands in the empty valley between them, describing a value that essentially never occurs. Anscombe's quartet makes this vivid: four datasets, identical means, utterly different realities.

The fix follows directly: report the statistics the mean threw away.
- **Spread** — standard deviation or IQR; an average without its variability is uninterpretable.
- **Shape/robust center** — the **median** and key percentiles (P50/P90/P99), which resist outliers and expose skew.
- **The distribution itself** — a histogram, whenever feasible.

The non-obvious insight: *which* average even makes sense depends on how the quantity combines. For rates and ratios you often want the **harmonic or geometric mean**; using the arithmetic mean on speeds or growth multipliers is simply the wrong operator, not just an imprecise one.
