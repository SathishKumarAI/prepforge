question: "Why can averages be misleading, and what should you report alongside them?"
topic: "Data Analytics"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"
sources: []
---

An average squeezes a whole crowd into one number, and that squeezing can hide the truth. Picture a small room where everyone earns a modest wage, then a billionaire walks in. The average income in the room now says everyone's rich, even though nobody's paycheck changed. One extreme value dragged the mean far from the typical person.

This happens whenever data is lopsided (incomes, website load times) or has outliers. The **median**, the middle value, ignores that billionaire and stays honest. There's also Simpson's paradox, where an overall average can point the opposite way from every subgroup within it.

So don't report the mean alone. Alongside it, show the **median** and **percentiles** (like p90, p95), a measure of **spread** (standard deviation or the range), the **number** of people included, and, when you can, the whole distribution as a histogram. One number rarely tells the full story.
