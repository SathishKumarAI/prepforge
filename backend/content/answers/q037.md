question: "What is k-fold cross-validation and why use it?"

k-fold cross-validation splits the data into `k` equal folds, then trains on `k−1` and validates on the held-out fold, rotating so every fold serves as validation exactly once. Average the `k` scores and you get a performance estimate that doesn't hinge on one lucky or unlucky split — plus a spread across folds that tells you how *stable* the model is.

Why bother instead of a single train/validation split? Two reasons. With limited data, a single split wastes information and gives a noisy estimate. And the per-fold variance is diagnostic: if your five folds score 0.72, 0.71, 0.90, 0.69, 0.73, you don't have a 0.75 model — you have an unstable one and one fold is hiding something (often leakage or a distribution shift). k=5 or k=10 is the usual sweet spot between bias, variance, and compute.

The variants exist for good reasons:

- **Stratified k-fold** preserves class ratios — mandatory for imbalanced classification.
- **Group k-fold** keeps all rows from the same user/entity in one fold so you don't leak identity across train and validation.
- **Time-series split** trains on the past and validates on the future — never shuffle temporal data.

The discipline that catches people: any preprocessing that learns from data — scaling, target encoding, feature selection — must be fit *inside* each fold via a pipeline. Fit it on the full set first and your CV scores are optimistically leaked.
