question: "Why can averages be misleading, and what should you report alongside them?"

The mean collapses a whole distribution into one number, and it's **highly sensitive to skew and outliers** — so on the data analytics staples (revenue, latency, session length, income) it routinely tells a story that isn't true. One whale customer or one 30-second request drags the average away from the typical experience.

The canonical example is **response times**. If your average API latency is 200ms, that sounds fine — but the average is dominated by a fast majority while a slow tail suffers. That's why in production we report **percentiles**: p50 (median) for the typical user, p95/p99 for the tail. The p99 might be 4 seconds while the mean says 200ms, and it's the p99 users who churn. "Average latency looks great" has masked more outages than almost any other metric.

What to report alongside a mean:

- **Median** — robust to outliers; the gap between mean and median instantly reveals skew.
- **A dispersion measure** — standard deviation, IQR, or min/max range. An average with no spread is half a story.
- **Percentiles or the full distribution** — a histogram often makes the point in one glance.
- **Sample size (n)** — an average of three data points isn't an average.

Two extra traps: **Simpson's paradox**, where an average reverses when you split by segment, and **averaging rates** (you can't average conversion rates across groups of different sizes — you must weight by volume). My rule: never ship a mean naked. At minimum pair it with the median and a percentile.
