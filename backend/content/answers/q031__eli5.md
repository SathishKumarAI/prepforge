question: "How does the k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN) algorithm work?"
topic: "Machine Learning"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"

**k-Nearest Neighbors** decides by asking the crowd right next to you. Imagine you move to a new street and want to guess whether your household will love gardening. The simplest bet: look at your **k closest neighbors** and go with the majority. That's exactly k-NN — to classify a new point, it measures the **distance** to every training example, grabs the k nearest, and takes a **vote** (or an average, when predicting numbers). It does no real "training" up front; all the work happens at question time, which makes predictions slow on big datasets. Two things matter a lot. First, **k**: too small and one weird neighbor sways you; too big and you blur across the whole town. Second, **scale your features** — otherwise a feature measured in thousands drowns out one measured in ones, distorting who even counts as "near."
