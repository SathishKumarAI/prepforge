The problem: partition unlabeled points into k groups so that each group is "tight." Formalize tightness as minimizing **within-cluster squared distance** to a representative point. That single objective *forces* everything about the algorithm.

The difficulty is that two unknowns are entangled: the cluster *assignments* and the cluster *centers*. Solve for either alone and the other is easy — this is a classic case for **coordinate descent (the EM pattern)**:

1. **Fix centers, optimize assignments:** assign each point to its nearest center (the choice that most reduces its squared error).
2. **Fix assignments, optimize centers:** set each center to the **mean** of its points — because the mean is provably the point minimizing summed squared distances.

Alternate until nothing changes. Each step can only *decrease* the objective, so it converges monotonically.

Two consequences fall straight out of the math. Because the objective uses **squared Euclidean distance**, k-means implicitly assumes clusters are round, similarly-sized, convex blobs — it fails on elongated or nested shapes. And because it's only guaranteed to descend, it converges to a **local** optimum sensitive to initialization (hence k-means++ seeding and multiple restarts).

The non-obvious insight: k-means is a hard-assignment, equal-variance special case of fitting a Gaussian mixture with EM. "Nearest mean" is what the general probabilistic framework collapses to when you assume spherical clusters and force each point into exactly one.
