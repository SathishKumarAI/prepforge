question: "What is a p-value and how do you interpret it?"

Imagine you flip a coin 10 times and get 9 heads. You start by assuming the coin is fair — that's the **null hypothesis**, the boring default that nothing special is going on. A p-value asks: "If the coin really were fair, how likely would I be to see a result this lopsided, or even more extreme?" A tiny p-value means your data would be very surprising under that fair-coin assumption, so you start doubting it. A big p-value means the result fits the boring assumption just fine.

Be careful: a small p-value does **not** tell you the probability the coin is fair, and it says nothing about how *big* the effect is. People set a cutoff (often 0.05) beforehand. And quietly testing many things until one "looks significant" is cheating.
