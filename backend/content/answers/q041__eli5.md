question: "How does a random forest work and why is it better than a single decision tree?"
topic: "Machine Learning"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"
sources: []

Imagine you have one health worry, and instead of trusting a single doctor, you ask a hundred doctors for a vote. But here's the twist: each doctor only saw a random slice of your history and was allowed to weigh only a few of your symptoms. That is a **random forest**: a team (an ensemble) of many decision trees, where every tree learns from a random sample of the data and, at each split, considers only a random handful of features.

A single deep decision tree is like one opinionated doctor who fixates on quirks and **overfits** (memorizes noise instead of the real pattern). Because the trees each make *different* mistakes, averaging their votes cancels the random errors out. The result is far steadier and more accurate, and it even tells you which features mattered most.
