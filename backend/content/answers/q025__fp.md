question: "Explain temperature, top-k, and top-p (nucleus) sampling in LLM decoding."

An LLM outputs, at each step, a probability distribution over the vocabulary (via softmax over logits). Decoding is the question: **how do we turn that distribution into a token?** Greedy argmax is deterministic but bland and repetitive; pure sampling honors the distribution but occasionally draws absurd low-probability tokens. The three knobs are different ways of *reshaping the distribution before sampling* to trade coherence against diversity.

**Temperature** rescales the logits by dividing by *T* before softmax. Because softmax is exponential, dividing logits sharpens (T<1, probabilities concentrate on the top tokens) or flattens (T>1, mass spreads out) the distribution. T→0 approaches greedy; high T approaches uniform. It's a global "confidence dial" — connected to statistical mechanics, where T literally controls entropy.

**Top-k** truncates: keep only the k highest-probability tokens, renormalize, sample. This hard-caps how far into the tail you can fall.

**Top-p (nucleus)** truncates *adaptively*: keep the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability ≥ p. When the model is confident, the nucleus is tiny; when uncertain, it's large.

The non-obvious insight: top-k uses a fixed *count* while top-p uses a fixed *probability mass* — top-p is usually better because it adapts the cutoff to the model's per-step uncertainty rather than imposing one arbitrary width everywhere.
