question: "What is the difference between mean, median, and mode, and when is each useful?"
topic: "Data Science"

They're three measures of central tendency. The **mean** is the arithmetic average, the **median** is the middle value when sorted, and the **mode** is the most frequent value. The practical difference is robustness: the mean is pulled hard by outliers and skew, the median shrugs them off, and the mode is the only one that works on categorical data.

Use the **mean** when data is roughly symmetric and you need something you can do further math on — it's the estimator that minimizes squared error and feeds cleanly into variance, regression, and t-tests. Use the **median** for skewed distributions: income, house prices, latency, session duration. Reporting "average salary $95k" when the median is $62k is technically true and functionally a lie, because a handful of executives dragged it up.

The **mode** matters for categoricals ("most common plan tier") and for spotting multimodality — if a distribution has two peaks, no single number describes it well and you should segment instead.

In production I always report **median and p95/p99** for latency, never the mean — a request that hangs for 30 seconds barely moves the mean but destroys user experience, and the tail is exactly what SLOs are written against. A quick tell: if mean ≈ median, the data is symmetric; if mean > median, it's right-skewed.
