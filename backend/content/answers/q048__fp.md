Suppose you must describe high-dimensional data with fewer numbers while losing as little as possible. "As little as possible" needs a definition — PCA chooses **variance**: the directions along which the data spreads most carry the most information, and flat directions are nearly constant, so discardable.

Formalize it. Center the data. Seek a unit direction `w` maximizing the variance of projections, `Var(Xw) = wᵀΣw`, where `Σ` is the covariance matrix. Maximizing a quadratic form under `‖w‖=1` is an eigenvalue problem: the answer is the top eigenvector of `Σ`, and the variance captured equals its eigenvalue `λ`. The next component is the top eigenvector *orthogonal* to the first, and so on. So PCA is simply the **eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix** — or equivalently the SVD of the data.

Two principles converge here. Geometrically, PCA finds the axes of the ellipsoid that best fits the cloud. Equivalently, it's the linear projection minimizing reconstruction error (Eckart–Young) — maximizing kept variance and minimizing lost variance are the same objective.

Non-obvious insight: the orthogonality of components means PCA *decorrelates* the data — the new features have diagonal covariance. It only captures *linear, second-order* structure, though; it is blind to any dependence that isn't correlation, which is why scaling and centering matter and why nonlinear manifolds defeat it.
