question: "Explain the bias-variance trade-off."
topic: "Machine Learning"

Picture teaching someone to shoot arrows. **Bias** is aiming at the wrong spot: every shot lands tightly grouped but far from the bullseye — your approach is too simplistic and *misses the real pattern* (**underfitting**), like drawing a straight line through curvy data. **Variance** is the jittery archer whose arrows scatter wildly because they overreact to every gust: they've memorized the practice range's quirks and fall apart on a new target (**overfitting**). The frustrating truth is a **trade-off**: making a model more complex to cut bias usually makes it twitchier (more variance), and simplifying to calm variance adds bias. Total error is roughly bias plus variance plus some unavoidable noise, so you hunt the **sweet spot** in between. Tools like regularization, cross-validation, and combining many models help — for example, averaging models tames variance while boosting attacks bias.
