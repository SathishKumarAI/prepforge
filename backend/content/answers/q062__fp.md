question: "What are Type I and Type II errors?"
topic: "Data Science"

Any decision rule that maps noisy evidence to a binary verdict (reject/keep the null) must be wrong in exactly two ways, because there are two ways reality and verdict can disagree. This isn't a list to memorize — it falls out of a 2×2 truth table.

- **Type I error (false positive):** you declare an effect that isn't there. Its rate is α, which *you choose* as the significance threshold.
- **Type II error (false negative):** you miss an effect that is real. Its rate is β; power = 1−β.

The first-principles tension: α and β are coupled through the same evidence distribution. Push the decision threshold to make false positives rarer, and you inevitably let more true effects slip past — you slide along a single ROC-like frontier. You cannot shrink both for free; the only way to *move the frontier itself* is to buy more information (larger sample, less variance, bigger true effect).

The insight people miss: which error is "worse" is not statistical but decision-theoretic — it depends on the asymmetric costs (a false cancer alarm vs. a missed tumor). Choosing α=0.05 is a value judgment about that cost ratio disguised as a convention, and power analysis is really just budgeting information to hit an acceptable β.
