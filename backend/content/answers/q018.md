It's the core dilemma of learning under uncertainty: do you **exploit** the action that currently looks best, or **explore** a less-certain action that might turn out better? Pure exploitation risks locking onto a local optimum because you never gathered enough evidence about the alternatives; pure exploration wastes reward on choices you already know are bad. The goal is to maximize *cumulative* reward over time, which means investing some short-term reward in information that pays off later.

The canonical setting is the multi-armed bandit, and the standard strategies scale from crude to principled:

- **ε-greedy** — act greedily but pick a random action with probability ε. Simple, effective, and what I reach for first. Decay ε over time so you explore early and exploit late.
- **Optimistic initialization** — start value estimates high so untried actions get pulled naturally.
- **UCB (Upper Confidence Bound)** — pick the action with the best *optimistic* estimate, adding a bonus for uncertainty; explores efficiently without randomness.
- **Thompson sampling** — sample from a posterior over action values and act greedily on the sample; usually the strongest performer in practice.

In production, the subtle failure I watch for is *non-stationarity*: if the environment drifts (user preferences, market), an agent that has stopped exploring will confidently serve a stale optimum. You need persistent, tuned exploration — a decayed ε that floors at a small positive value, or a sliding-window estimate — rather than letting exploration go to zero.
