You have data and a *family* of possible generating distributions indexed by parameters θ (a coin's bias, a Gaussian's mean/variance). The question: which θ should you believe? MLE gives a principled answer via inversion — **pick the θ that makes the data you actually observed most probable.** If a parameter setting would render your observations wildly unlikely, it's a poor explanation; the best explanation is the one under which reality was least surprising.

Formally, define the likelihood L(θ) = P(data | θ). Assuming independent samples, this is a *product* of per-point probabilities. Products are numerically nasty and hard to differentiate, so we maximize the **log-likelihood** — a sum — which has the same argmax because log is monotonic. Set the derivative to zero and solve.

Why this is deep: MLE is where statistics meets information theory. Maximizing log-likelihood is *equivalent* to minimizing the KL divergence between the true data distribution and your model — MLE literally chooses the model closest to reality in information terms. It also explains why squared-error loss (Gaussian noise) and cross-entropy loss (categorical) aren't arbitrary: they're negative log-likelihoods. Training a neural net is usually MLE in disguise.

The insight most miss: MLE has *no prior* — it trusts the data completely. With little data it overfits (a coin flipped twice landing heads twice → "bias = 1"), which is exactly the gap Bayesian estimation fills with a prior.
