question: "Why does correlation not imply causation?"

Every summer, ice-cream sales spike *and* drownings spike, right alongside each other. Does ice cream cause drowning? Obviously not — a hidden third factor, **hot weather**, drives both. That's the heart of why **correlation doesn't imply causation**: two things moving together doesn't mean one makes the other happen.

Several traps create this illusion. A **confounder** (like the heat) secretly drives both variables at once. **Reverse causation** flips the arrow — maybe the effect is actually the cause. And with enough variables or tiny samples, you get pure **coincidence** — spurious patterns that mean nothing.

To actually prove cause, you usually need a **randomized experiment** (an A/B test) that isolates the change, or careful **causal inference** methods when experiments aren't possible. The practical danger: if you treat a mere correlation as if it were causal and act on it, you can make expensive, embarrassingly wrong decisions.
