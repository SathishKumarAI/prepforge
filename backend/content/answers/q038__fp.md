Accuracy fails the moment classes are imbalanced: predict "not fraud" always and you're 99.9% accurate and useless. The fix is to stop asking "how often am I right?" and start asking about the **two different mistakes**, which have different costs.

A positive prediction can be wrong (false positive); a real positive can be missed (false negative). Precision and recall isolate these:

- **Precision** = of everything I *flagged* positive, what fraction truly is? It answers "**can I trust a positive prediction?**" and is hurt by false positives.
- **Recall** = of all *actual* positives, what fraction did I catch? It answers "**did I miss anything?**" and is hurt by false negatives.

They trade off because both share the same knob: the decision threshold. Lower it to catch more positives (↑recall) and you also flag more junk (↓precision). This tension is intrinsic — you can't maximize both by fiat.

Which to prioritize follows from **asymmetric error costs**:

- Missing a positive is catastrophic (cancer screening, fraud) → maximize **recall**.
- A false alarm is costly (spam filter deleting real mail, expensive intervention) → maximize **precision**.

**F1** is their harmonic mean — chosen over the arithmetic mean because it punishes imbalance: 100% precision but 1% recall scores near 0, not 50%.

The non-obvious insight: F1 quietly assumes false positives and false negatives cost *equally*. If they don't, optimize a threshold to your actual cost ratio (or use Fβ) instead.
