Both are supervised learning, and the only real difference is the target: classification predicts a **discrete label**, regression predicts a **continuous number**. Spam-or-not is classification; predicting a house price is regression.

That distinction cascades through the whole pipeline. Classification uses cross-entropy loss and gets measured with accuracy, precision/recall, F1, or AUC. Regression uses squared or absolute error and gets measured with RMSE, MAE, or R². Same algorithm family often does both — a decision tree, gradient-boosted trees, or a neural net just swaps the output layer and loss.

The line blurs in useful ways. A classifier usually emits a probability (0.87) before you threshold it into a class, so under the hood it's doing something regression-like. And ordinal problems — star ratings 1–5 — sit awkwardly between the two; treating them as pure classification throws away the ordering, treating them as regression pretends the gaps are equal.

In production the mistake I see most is picking the wrong framing for the business question. "Predict revenue" sounds like regression, but if the decision is really "will this account churn or not," you want a calibrated classifier whose threshold you can tune to the cost of false positives versus false negatives. Match the output type to the decision being made, not to the raw column dtype.
