Linear regression models the target as a weighted sum of features, `ŷ = β₀ + β₁x₁ + … + βₙxₙ`, and finds the coefficients that minimize the sum of squared residuals. Ordinary least squares has a closed-form solution (the normal equations); at scale or with regularization you solve it with gradient descent instead.

The coefficients are the whole appeal: `βᵢ` is the expected change in `y` per unit change in `xᵢ`, holding others fixed. That interpretability is why it's still the first model I reach for on tabular data — it's a baseline and a sanity check before anything fancier.

The assumptions matter mostly for *inference* (trusting p-values and confidence intervals), less for raw prediction:

- **Linearity** — the relationship really is linear in the parameters.
- **Independent errors** — violated by time series and clustered data.
- **Homoscedasticity** — constant residual variance; funnel-shaped residual plots signal trouble.
- **Normally distributed residuals** — needed for valid inference, not for the point estimates.
- **Low multicollinearity** — correlated features blow up coefficient variance (check VIF).

In production the failure I see most is multicollinearity making coefficients flip sign and become unstable — the model still predicts fine, but any story you tell about "feature X drives Y" is nonsense. Plot residuals, check VIF, and reach for Ridge when features are correlated.
