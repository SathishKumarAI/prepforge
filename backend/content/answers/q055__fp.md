The mistake is to treat missingness as a nuisance to be filled. It's first a *question*: **why** is the value absent? Rubin's taxonomy is the key because the mechanism determines what's safe to do.

- **MCAR** (missing completely at random): absence is independent of everything. Dropping rows is unbiased — you just lose power.
- **MAR** (missing at random): absence depends on *observed* variables (e.g., older people skip income). You can recover the value from the others, so model-based imputation is valid.
- **MNAR** (missing not at random): absence depends on the *unobserved value itself* (high earners hide income). No imputation from observed data is unbiased — the missingness *is* the signal.

This tells you why naïve fixes fail. Mean imputation shrinks variance and destroys correlations — you've injected fake certainty at the center. Dropping rows under MAR or MNAR biases the sample toward whoever answers.

Better tools respect the mechanism: multiple imputation draws several plausible values and *propagates the uncertainty* into final estimates; model-based imputation (kNN, MICE, iterative regression) uses the correlations you actually have.

Non-obvious insight: **missingness is itself data.** Adding a binary "was_missing" indicator often captures MNAR signal that any imputed value would erase — the fact that someone declined to answer can be more predictive than the answer.
