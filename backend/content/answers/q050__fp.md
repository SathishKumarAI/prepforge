The entire point of a test set is to *simulate the future*: estimate how the model performs on data it will genuinely never have seen at prediction time. Leakage is any way that information which won't be available at prediction time leaks into training — so your evaluation measures a world that can't exist, and the number lies.

Two flavors follow from this definition. **Target leakage:** a feature encodes the answer, e.g., "was_refunded" predicting fraud — a value only known *after* the outcome. **Train/test contamination:** statistics computed over the whole dataset (a scaler's mean, an imputation value, feature selection, target encoding) absorb test-set information before the split. The model then implicitly "knows" the test distribution.

The unifying principle is a **temporal/causal boundary**: at inference you stand at a moment in time and know only the past. Any quantity that depends on the future, or on the pooled dataset, violates that boundary.

Prevention follows mechanically: split *first*, then fit every transformation on the training fold only and *apply* it to validation/test — which is exactly why scikit-learn Pipelines exist. For time series, split chronologically, never randomly.

Non-obvious insight: leakage's signature is *suspiciously excellent* offline metrics that collapse in production. If your model seems too good, distrust it — you've probably let it peek at the answer.
