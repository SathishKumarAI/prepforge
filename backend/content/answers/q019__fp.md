An MDP is the minimal mathematical object needed to make *sequential decision-making under uncertainty* tractable. The challenge: an agent takes actions over time, outcomes are stochastic, and rewards are delayed — so a good choice now depends on an unbounded history of consequences. That's intractable in general. The MDP makes it solvable by imposing one powerful simplifying assumption.

Formally it's a tuple (S, A, P, R, γ): states, actions, a transition function P(s'|s,a), a reward R(s,a), and a discount factor γ. The crucial ingredient is the **Markov property**: the next state and reward depend *only on the current state and action*, not on the full history. Why does this matter so much? Because it means the current state is a **sufficient statistic** for the future — everything relevant about the past is compressed into "where you are now." That collapses an intractable dependence on history into a fixed-size problem.

This is exactly what makes optimality *recursive*. Because the future is conditionally independent of the past given the present, the value of a state can be written in terms of the values of successor states — the **Bellman equation**. Dynamic programming, value iteration, and Q-learning all rest on this recursion.

The non-obvious insight: the Markov property isn't a claim about reality — it's a *design constraint on state representation*. If your state isn't Markov, you fix it by enriching the state (adding history) until it becomes so.
