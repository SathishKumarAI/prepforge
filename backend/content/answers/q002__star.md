**Situation** At a chatbot company, our marketing team wanted to claim our support bot "passed the Turing Test," and I had to sanity-check that claim before it went into a press release that legal was already reviewing.

**Task** My job was to explain what the Turing Test actually measures and design an honest evaluation so we didn't overstate anything.

**Action** I explained that the Turing Test, proposed by Alan Turing in 1950, measures whether a machine's conversational behavior is indistinguishable from a human's to a blind human judge; it tests imitation of behavior, not real understanding or consciousness. Rather than claim a "pass," I set up a controlled study: 30 evaluators chatted with either our bot or a human agent and guessed which was which. I tracked the misclassification rate as our metric.

**Result** Judges guessed correctly 78% of the time, so we clearly hadn't "passed." We reworded the press release to "human-like responses" and avoided a credibility risk. I learned the Turing Test is about perceived indistinguishability, not intelligence itself.
