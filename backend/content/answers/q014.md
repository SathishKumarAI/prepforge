RAG grounds an LLM in external documents so it answers from evidence you control rather than from whatever's frozen in its weights. Pipeline: chunk your documents, embed each chunk into a vector, store them in a vector index; at query time embed the question, retrieve the top-k most similar chunks (ANN search — FAISS, pgvector, Elasticsearch), stuff them into the prompt as context, and let the model answer over them.

The reason it's everywhere: it fixes the two biggest LLM weaknesses at once — stale knowledge and hallucination — without retraining, and it lets you cite sources and use private data. Update the index, and the model "knows" new facts instantly.

Where it actually breaks in production, in my experience, is almost never the LLM — it's **retrieval**. If the right chunk isn't in the top-k, the model can't use it, and it'll happily hallucinate instead. So the engineering effort goes into chunking strategy (too big dilutes relevance, too small loses context), a **reranker** (a cross-encoder) on top of vector search, and **hybrid** retrieval combining semantic vectors with keyword/BM25 for exact terms like product codes.

I evaluate the two halves separately: retrieval with recall@k and hit rate, generation with faithfulness/groundedness checks. And I've learned to distinguish RAG from fine-tuning cleanly: RAG is for *knowledge that changes*, fine-tuning is for *behavior and format*. They compose well; they don't substitute for each other.
