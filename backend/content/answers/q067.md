Skewness measures the asymmetry of a distribution. **Right (positive) skew** has a long tail toward high values and mean > median — think income, prices, counts, session durations. **Left (negative) skew** is the mirror image. As a rough gauge, |skew| > 1 is substantial; near 0 is roughly symmetric.

Why care? Several common models assume symmetry or bounded influence. Linear/logistic regression, distance-based methods (k-NN, k-means), and PCA all get distorted when a heavy tail lets extreme points dominate. Skewed *targets* also break the constant-variance assumption of OLS.

Fixes, in order of what I reach for:

- **Log transform** (`log1p` to handle zeros) — the workhorse for right-skewed positive data; it compresses the tail and often makes the feature near-normal.
- **Square root** for milder skew or count data; **Box-Cox** (positive data) or **Yeo-Johnson** (handles zero/negative) to *learn* the best power transform.
- **Quantile/rank transforms** when you just need a uniform or normal shape and don't care about interpretability.

Two caveats from production. First, **tree-based models don't need any of this** — they split on order, so monotonic transforms are wasted effort there; I only transform for linear/distance models. Second, fit the transform on **training data only** and apply it to validation/test, or you leak. And when you log-transform the target, remember predictions come back in log space — you must exponentiate (and correct for the transformation bias) before reporting.
