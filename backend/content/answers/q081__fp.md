Start from the physics of the workload. An operational database (OLTP) exists to serve millions of tiny, concurrent writes and point-lookups—"insert this order," "fetch user 42." A warehouse exists to answer a few enormous questions—"sum revenue by region over three years." These access patterns are *physically antagonistic*: optimizing for one degrades the other.

Why must they diverge? A transactional DB stores data **row-wise** and normalizes it, so a single record touches few pages and stays consistent under concurrent writes. An analytical query, though, scans billions of rows but only a handful of *columns*. So warehouses store data **column-wise**: each column is contiguous, highly compressible (similar values cluster), and you read only the columns you need. The same query that would thrash an OLTP system becomes a streaming scan.

The deeper principle is **separation by information granularity**. Warehouses deliberately denormalize and pre-aggregate, trading write-flexibility and storage for read-throughput—the opposite of a DB's bargain.

The non-obvious insight: a warehouse is not a "bigger database." It's a different point on the fundamental trade-off between *write agility* and *read throughput*—which is why you copy data into it (via ETL) rather than querying production directly.
