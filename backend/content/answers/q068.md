question: "What is the difference between Pearson and Spearman correlation?"
topic: "Data Science"

---

Both quantify how two variables move together on a −1 to +1 scale, but they measure different things. **Pearson** measures the strength of a *linear* relationship using the raw values. **Spearman** is Pearson computed on the *ranks* of the values, so it measures any *monotonic* relationship — whether one variable consistently increases with the other, linear or not.

That difference drives when to use each. Reach for **Spearman** when: the relationship is monotonic but curved (e.g., y = x³ gives Spearman = 1 but Pearson < 1); the data has outliers (ranks are robust — one extreme point barely moves Spearman but can dominate Pearson); the variables are ordinal (satisfaction ratings 1–5); or the data is heavily skewed and non-normal. Use **Pearson** when the relationship is genuinely linear and you want to reason about effect size in original units.

Two things I emphasize in production. First, **correlation only captures monotonic (Pearson: linear) structure** — a perfect U-shape gives ~0 on both, yet the variables are tightly related. Always plot the scatter; don't trust a single coefficient. Second, both are pairwise and say nothing about causation or about conditional relationships that appear only when controlling for a third variable. For non-monotonic or nonlinear dependence, mutual information is the more general tool. A quick heuristic: if Pearson ≪ Spearman, suspect a nonlinear-but-monotonic relationship or outliers.
