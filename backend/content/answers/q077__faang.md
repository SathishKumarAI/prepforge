**Clarify** — Confirm we mean a Key Performance Indicator — a metric tied to a business objective — and distinguish it from a plain metric. Ask what goal or team the KPI would serve.

**Approach** — Define it, then lay out the criteria that separate a good KPI from vanity numbers.

**Depth** — A KPI is a quantifiable measure of progress toward a specific business objective; the "key" means it's one of the few that actually drive decisions. A good KPI is: **aligned** with a strategic goal; **actionable** — a team can move it; **measurable** and clearly defined so there's no ambiguity; **specific with a target and timeframe** (SMART); and ideally a **leading** indicator that predicts outcomes, not just a lagging one that reports them. It should resist gaming and pair with guardrail metrics.

**Edge cases** — Vanity metrics (raw page views, cumulative signups) look good but don't inform decisions. Single KPIs get gamed — optimizing "calls handled" can tank quality; pair with counter-metrics. Averages hide distributions; consider percentiles.

**Optimize & communicate** — Limit to a handful of KPIs per team, define them precisely in a metric dictionary, and balance leading and lagging plus guardrails. Narrate: "A good KPI changes what someone does tomorrow" — that action-orientation is exactly the signal.
