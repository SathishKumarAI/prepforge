Feature engineering is turning raw data into inputs that expose the signal your model can actually learn from. On tabular data it's often the difference between a mediocre model and a great one — a linear model with the right features beats a tuned gradient-boosting model with lazy ones. Models can only learn from what you show them.

The bread and butter: encoding categoricals (one-hot, target, or ordinal), scaling numerics, building ratios and interactions, binning, and extracting structure — day-of-week and is-holiday from a timestamp, TF-IDF or embeddings from text, aggregations (a customer's 30-day spend) from event logs. Domain knowledge is the multiplier here; the best features I've built came from a subject-matter expert explaining what actually drives the outcome, not from any library.

Two caveats I hammer on. First, **leakage** is the silent killer: if a feature encodes information you won't have at prediction time — or that's derived from the target — your offline metrics look spectacular and production collapses. I've watched a "fraud score" feature that was literally computed after the fraud label was assigned sail through review and tank in prod. Compute features using only data available at decision time, and fit any target-based encoding inside cross-validation folds.

Deep learning learns representations automatically and reduces manual work for images/text, but for tabular problems thoughtful features still win, and they make models cheaper, faster, and easier to explain.
