The multiple comparisons problem is that when you run many hypothesis tests, the chance of at least one false positive skyrockets even if nothing is real. At α = 0.05, each test has a 5% false-positive rate — but run 20 independent tests and the probability of *at least one* spurious "significant" result is 1 − 0.95²⁰ ≈ 64%. Run 100 and you're essentially guaranteed several. This is exactly the *xkcd jelly-bean* comic: test 20 colors, one comes up "linked to acne," publish that one.

Corrections fall into two philosophies:

- **Control the Family-Wise Error Rate (FWER)** — the probability of *any* false positive. **Bonferroni** is the blunt instrument: test each at α/m. Simple and conservative; with many tests it kills real effects (low power). **Holm-Bonferroni** is uniformly more powerful and just as safe.
- **Control the False Discovery Rate (FDR)** — the expected *proportion* of discoveries that are false. **Benjamini-Hochberg** is the standard. It's less conservative and the right choice when you're screening many hypotheses (genomics, thousands of features) and can tolerate some false positives among your hits.

In production this bites hardest in **A/B testing and metric dashboards**. Slicing one experiment by 15 segments, or peeking at 30 secondary metrics, is silent multiple testing — you'll "find" wins that don't replicate. My rules: pre-register the primary metric, treat everything else as exploratory, and apply BH correction to segment scans. Choosing metrics *after* seeing the data (the garden of forking paths) is the version no correction fully saves you from.
