question: "What are Slowly Changing Dimensions (SCDs) and what are the common types?"

The problem is a collision between two truths. Facts are *events*—stamped to a moment ("$100 sale on March 3"). Dimensions are *attributes of entities*—a customer's city, a product's price tier—and those attributes **change over time**. When a customer moves from Ohio to Texas, how should past sales be attributed? Answering that forces a design choice, and SCD "types" enumerate the possible answers.

- **Type 0** — never change; the attribute is frozen (original signup date).
- **Type 1** — overwrite. Keep only the current value; history is lost. Cheap, but past reports now silently rewrite themselves.
- **Type 2** — add a new row per change, with `valid_from`/`valid_to` dates and a "current" flag. Full history preserved; the entity now spans multiple rows keyed by a surrogate key.
- **Type 3** — add a column (`previous_value`) to keep limited history—only the last state.

The unifying principle is that a dimension is really a **function of two arguments: entity *and* time**, `attribute(entity, t)`. Type 1 collapses the time axis; Type 2 materializes it. The choice is a trade between storage/complexity and the ability to answer "what was true *then*?"

The non-obvious insight: Type 2 is why warehouses use **surrogate keys** instead of natural business keys. One customer becomes several dimension rows over their history, so the fact table must point at the *version* that was current at the event's timestamp—preserving historical accuracy that a natural key would destroy.
