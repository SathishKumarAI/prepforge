A join answers a set-theoretic question: given two tables and a matching condition, which combined rows do we keep? Start from the raw material — the Cartesian product, every row of A paired with every row of B. A join is that product *filtered* by the ON condition. The four types are four policies for **rows that find no match**.

- **INNER JOIN:** keep only pairs where the condition holds; unmatched rows vanish. The strict intersection — the default when you want confirmed relationships only.
- **LEFT JOIN:** keep every left row; where the right has no match, NULL-fill its columns. Preserves the left's completeness — "all customers *and* their orders, including customers with none."
- **RIGHT JOIN:** the mirror image, preserving the right table. Rarely used, since reordering lets you always write LEFT.
- **FULL OUTER JOIN:** keep everything from both sides, NULL-filling wherever either lacks a match. The union — used to reconcile two sources.

The unifying insight: the whole family reduces to *one* choice — "must a row have a partner to survive?" — asked independently of each side. **NULLs are the signature of unmatched rows**, which is why LEFT JOIN + `WHERE right.key IS NULL` is the idiom for "rows in A with no counterpart in B" (an anti-join) — a pattern INNER JOIN can never express.
