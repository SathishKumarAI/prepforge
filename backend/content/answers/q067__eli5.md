question: "What is skewness, and how do you deal with skewed features?"

Picture a neighborhood where almost everyone earns a modest salary, but a few tech founders rake in millions. Chart that, and most people bunch up on the left while a long thin tail stretches far to the right. That lopsidedness is **skewness** — a distribution that isn't symmetric. With this "right skew," the average gets dragged upward by the extreme few, so it sits higher than the typical (middle) person.

Why fix it? Many models expect roughly balanced data, and those extreme values can dominate and distort results. Common cures: take the **logarithm** of the values, which squashes the huge ones and pulls the tail in; or use square-root or Box-Cox transforms; or cap the wildest values. Tree-based models don't mind skew at all.

Whatever you do, check afterward with a quick histogram to confirm the shape actually got more balanced.
