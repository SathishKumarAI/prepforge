**Marketing attribution** is the practice of assigning credit for a conversion to the marketing touchpoints that led to it. A user rarely converts from one ad — they see a Facebook ad, click a Google search result days later, then convert from an email. Attribution decides how the credit for that sale is split across those touches, which directly drives budget allocation.

The main **rule-based (heuristic) models**:

- **Last-touch** — 100% credit to the final touchpoint. Simple, default in most tools, but systematically over-credits bottom-of-funnel channels (branded search, retargeting) and starves the channels that created demand.
- **First-touch** — all credit to the initial touch. The mirror bias: over-credits awareness, ignores what closed the deal.
- **Linear** — equal credit to every touch. Fair but naive; treats a throwaway impression like a decisive click.
- **Time-decay** — more credit to touches closer to conversion. Reasonable when recency matters.
- **Position-based (U-shaped)** — typically 40% first, 40% last, 20% spread across the middle. A pragmatic compromise honoring both discovery and closing.

Then there's **data-driven / algorithmic attribution**, which uses the actual conversion data (often Shapley values or Markov chains on real paths) to estimate each channel's marginal contribution rather than a fixed rule. It's the most defensible but needs volume and clean cross-device tracking.

The honest caveat I always give stakeholders: all of these are correlational. With privacy changes (iOS ATT, cookie deprecation) tracking is increasingly leaky, and the only causal answer comes from **incrementality testing** — geo holdouts or randomized experiments — not from any attribution model.
