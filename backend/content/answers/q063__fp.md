The fundamental problem: you want the *causal* effect of a change, but you only ever observe one version of reality per user — you never see the counterfactual. A/B testing solves this by **randomization**, which makes the treatment and control groups statistically identical in expectation on *every* variable, observed or not. That is the whole trick: random assignment converts correlation into causation by breaking any link between group membership and confounders.

Design then follows from protecting that guarantee and from managing noise:

1. **One hypothesis, one primary metric.** Decide the metric and expected effect *before* looking, or you'll fish for significance.
2. **Randomize at the right unit** (user, not session) to avoid contamination and interference.
3. **Power the test first:** sample size scales like variance / effect², so smaller effects cost quadratically more data. This determines runtime *before* launch — peeking early inflates false positives.
4. **Run a full cycle** (weekly seasonality, novelty effects) and pre-commit the stopping point.
5. **Sanity-check** with an A/A test and sample-ratio mismatch.

The insight most miss: statistical significance ≠ decision-worthiness. You test against a *practical* minimum detectable effect, and you weigh the effect distribution against implementation cost — a real but tiny lift can be not worth shipping.
