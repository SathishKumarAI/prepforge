Start from the core problem: you observe an effect in noisy data and must decide whether it reflects a real signal or just sampling luck. You cannot directly compute "probability the effect is real" without priors, so instead you ask a question you *can* answer: *if there were no effect, how surprising is this data?*

The p-value formalizes that surprise. Assume the null hypothesis (no effect) is exactly true; the p-value is the probability, under that assumed world, of observing a test statistic **at least as extreme** as the one you got. Small p means your data is rare in a world where nothing is happening — so that world becomes hard to sustain.

The deep principle is proof by contradiction under uncertainty: we can't prove nothingness false, but we can measure how badly it fits.

The non-obvious insight most people miss: a p-value is **not** the probability the null is true, nor the probability of a false positive. It is a property of the *data given the hypothesis*, not the hypothesis given the data — those differ by Bayes' theorem and by the prior. This is why a p=0.04 result on an implausible claim is often still wrong.
