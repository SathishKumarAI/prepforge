The cleanest way to separate them is by *what signal the model learns from*.

**Supervised learning** learns from labeled input-output pairs — you have the ground truth. Classification (spam/not-spam) and regression (predict price) live here. Most production ML is supervised because labels, when you have them, give the strongest signal. The catch is labels are expensive; I've spent more project time on labeling pipelines and inter-annotator agreement than on modeling.

**Unsupervised learning** has no labels — you're finding structure. Clustering for customer segmentation, PCA/UMAP for dimensionality reduction, anomaly detection. The hard part is evaluation: there's no accuracy number, so you're stuck with silhouette scores plus a domain expert eyeballing whether clusters mean anything. In practice I use it more for *exploration* and feature generation than as an end deliverable.

**Reinforcement learning** learns from a reward signal through trial and error — an agent takes actions, gets rewards, and optimizes cumulative return over time. AlphaGo, robotics, RLHF for LLMs. It's powerful when you can't label the right answer but can score outcomes, but it's sample-hungry and unstable to train.

A useful nuance: the boundaries blur. **Self-supervised** learning (how LLMs pretrain) generates labels from the data itself — next-token prediction — giving supervised-style signal without human labels. That's arguably the most important paradigm in modern AI, and it doesn't fit cleanly in the classic three buckets.
