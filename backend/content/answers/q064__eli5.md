question: "What is the Central Limit Theorem and why does it matter?"
topic: "Data Science"
model: "Claude Code (authored)"
sources: []
---

Roll a single die and the outcomes are flat — 1 through 6 are equally likely, nothing bell-shaped about it. But now roll thirty dice and write down the average. Do that over and over, and those averages pile up into a lovely **bell curve** centered on 3.5. That's the **Central Limit Theorem**: no matter how weird the original data looks, the *average* of a big enough sample behaves like a normal, bell-shaped distribution.

Two bonuses: the averages center on the true population average, and they spread less as your sample grows, so bigger samples give steadier estimates. "Big enough" is often around 30, more if the data is very lopsided.

Why care? It's the reason we can build confidence intervals and run tests about averages even when the raw data is nowhere near a bell curve. It's the quiet engine behind most everyday statistics.
